# Should We Seek Signs From God?

## A Study on Coincidence, Confirmation, and Faith

*Primary Translation: Berean Standard Bible (BSB)*

---

## What's Actually Going On Here

Most of us have heard someone describe an experience like this: a particular bird shows up at just the right moment, a number keeps repeating, a dream feels too vivid to ignore, or something happens right after a prayer that seems to answer it exactly. The person telling the story usually isn't being dishonest or foolish — they're describing something that felt meaningful, often during a hard or emotional time, and they're reaching for language to make sense of it. "God sign," "god-wink," "coincidence that wasn't really a coincidence" — these are common ways people describe it.

This study isn't about mocking that impulse or accusing anyone of doing something terrible. It's about asking a more basic question: does Scripture actually describe God working this way as the normal pattern for how He communicates with His people? And if not, what is this impulse, where does it come from, and what does Scripture say we should do with it?

As we'll see by the end of this study, the honest answer is that this impulse is far more universal — and far more natural — than most of us realize. That includes the person writing this.

---

## What Scripture Actually Says

Before getting into examples, it helps to look directly at what the Bible says about seeking and interpreting signs. A few of these passages use words that aren't part of everyday vocabulary, so each one includes an explanation of what's actually being said.

### Jesus and the Request for a Sign

> **Matthew 12:38-39 (BSB)** *"Then some of the scribes and Pharisees said to Him, 'Teacher, we want to see a sign from You.' But He answered and said to them, 'An evil and adulterous generation craves for a sign; and yet no sign will be given to it but the sign of Jonah the prophet.'"*

> **Matthew 16:1, 4 (BSB)** *"The Pharisees and Sadducees came up, and testing Jesus, they asked Him to show them a sign from heaven... 'An evil and adulterous generation seeks after a sign; and a sign will not be given it, except the sign of Jonah.'"*

**ἐπιζητέω (*epizēteō* (eh-pee-zay-TAY-oh))** — to seek out, search for, or demand. It describes an active pursuit, not a passive noticing.

The religious leaders in this story weren't asking for something unreasonable by the world's standards — they wanted proof before they would believe. Jesus doesn't respond by giving them what they asked for. He treats the request itself — "show me something and then I'll believe" — as the problem. That's worth sitting with, because it's the exact opposite of how a lot of popular Christian teaching frames sign-seeking today.

**Observation:** Jesus never responds to a demand for a sign by producing one. The pattern in the Gospels is the reverse — He repeatedly calls the demand for proof a symptom of unbelief, not a step toward faith. It is also worth noting that Jesus is addressing hardened religious leaders who had already witnessed numerous miracles — not an ordinary believer sincerely asking for God's guidance. The principle still holds, but the context matters: not every request for confirmation in Scripture is condemned. What Scripture consistently condemns is demanding proof before believing, and cultivating the habit of scanning ordinary life for ongoing personal revelation beyond what God has already spoken.

### Divination, Omens, and Reading Hidden Messages Into Things

> **Deuteronomy 18:9-12 (BSB)** *"When you enter the land that the LORD your God is giving you, do not imitate the detestable ways of the nations there. Let no one be found among you who sacrifices his son or daughter in the fire, practices divination or conjury, interprets omens, practices sorcery, casts spells, consults a medium or spiritist, or inquires of the dead. For whoever does these things is detestable to the LORD. And because of these detestable things, the LORD your God is driving out the nations before you."*

**קֹסֵם (*qosem* (KOH-sem))** — the word rendered "divination" or "witchcraft" depending on the translation — someone who reads messages or guidance from the gods through signs, objects, or rituals.

**תּוֹעֵבָה (*to'evah* (toh-ay-VAH))** — detestable/abomination — a strong word, used for things God finds deeply offensive, not merely unwise or old-fashioned.

This passage needs to be unpacked, because words like "witchcraft," "divination," and "omen" sound like ancient history and not like anything an ordinary modern person would do. But here's what these words actually describe: in the ancient world, people watched for hidden messages from the gods in ordinary things — the way birds flew, the patterns in the organs of a sacrificed animal, dreams, unusual events, lucky and unlucky numbers, signs in the sky. The belief was that if you paid close enough attention, the universe (or the gods) would communicate with you through these everyday occurrences.

God's instruction to Israel wasn't "do this, but only if it's really Me speaking." It was a flat "don't do this at all" — and the reason given is that this is exactly what the nations around Israel did, and it's described as one of the reasons God removed those nations from the land. This wasn't framed as a minor cultural difference. It was framed as something God finds detestable.

**Observation:** The issue in Deuteronomy 18 isn't that the ancient practices used birds or animal organs specifically — it's the underlying impulse toward seeking hidden revelation from God beyond what He has spoken. That impulse — scanning ordinary events for personal divine messages — closely resembles the mindset behind the pagan omen-reading Scripture warns against, even if the practices themselves differ. The method has changed; the underlying logic has not.

### Faith That Doesn't Require Proof

> **2 Corinthians 5:7 (BSB)** *"for we walk by faith, not by sight."*

> **John 20:27-29 (BSB)** *"Then He said to Thomas, 'Reach here with your finger, and see My hands; and reach here your hand and put it into My side; and do not be unbelieving, but believing.' Thomas answered and said to Him, 'My Lord and my God!' Jesus said to him, 'Because you have seen Me, have you believed? Blessed are they who did not see, and yet believed.'"*

A note on context: this exchange is sometimes confused with the Beatitudes of the Sermon on the Mount, but it takes place in an entirely different setting — Jesus appearing to the disciples after the resurrection, specifically addressing Thomas's demand to physically see and touch the wounds before he would believe. The principle fits alongside 2 Corinthians 5:7: both describe a faith that does not depend on first receiving physical proof or confirmation.

**Observation:** Scripture consistently describes the normal Christian life as one of trusting what God has already revealed in His Word — not one of collecting ongoing proofs to keep that trust going. A faith that needs a new confirmation every week to stay standing is not the kind of faith these passages describe.

### Testing What We're Told

> **1 Thessalonians 5:20-21 (BSB)** *"do not despise prophetic utterances. But examine everything carefully; hold fast to that which is good."*

> **2 Corinthians 11:14 (BSB)** *"No wonder, for even Satan disguises himself as an angel of light."*

These two passages work together. The first says we're not supposed to dismiss everything out of hand — but we are supposed to test it. The second is a sobering reminder of what the standard for that test can't be: how something feels. Something can feel peaceful, timely, encouraging, and "right" and still not be from God. That means the feeling itself can never be the proof. There has to be something outside the feeling — namely, Scripture — that the experience is tested against.

**Observation:** "Test everything" only works if there's a fixed standard to test against. For the Christian, that standard is Scripture — not the emotional weight of the experience itself.

## The Real Test: Confirmed, or Constructed?

Here's where all of this comes together into one practical question.

Look at the signs God actually gives in Scripture — the rainbow after the flood, the blood on the doorposts at Passover, the empty tomb. In every case, the sign comes attached to something God had already said. The rainbow confirmed a promise God had already spoken. The blood on the doorposts confirmed instructions God had already given. The empty tomb confirmed what Jesus had already told His disciples would happen. The sign never showed up first, with the meaning assigned afterward. The word came first, and the sign confirmed it.

So here's the question to ask about any experience that feels like a sign:

*Did God say this somewhere in His Word first, and this event confirms it? Or am I the one assigning meaning to something that happened, after the fact?*

With God, the first question — "could He do this?" — always technically has the answer "yes." He can do anything. That's never really in dispute. But "He could" is a very different claim from "He did, and here's how I know." The second question is the one that actually matters, and it's the one this study is really about.

## How This Shows Up in Everyday Life

The patterns below aren't hypothetical. They're common, widely shared, and often taught explicitly within Christian circles — sometimes with entire books and ministries built around them. None of these examples are about any one person. They're about a way of thinking that's become so normal it rarely gets questioned.

### 1. Numbers

Repeating numbers — seeing "111" or "333" on a clock, a receipt, or a hotel room door; numbers that match a birthday or an anniversary showing up unexpectedly; the number three appearing in a way that gets connected to the Trinity. Often called "angel numbers" in broader spiritual culture, and the same framework gets carried into Christian circles with different labels.

### 2. Animals and Nature

A specific bird — cardinals are especially common, often tied to the belief that a cardinal appearing after a loss is a visit from a loved one or a message from God. Also: butterflies, rainbows appearing at a meaningful moment, or weather that seems to "respond" to a prayer or an emotional moment.

### 3. "God Winks" and "Godcidences"

This is worth naming directly because it's not a fringe idea — it's an entire framework that gets taught, often with books, sermons, and ministries built around the concept. The basic idea is that small, ordinary coincidences (running into the right person, a song playing at just the right moment, the same word or topic showing up twice in one day) are personal messages or "winks" from God, distinct from major miracles but still treated as direct communication.

The appeal is understandable — it makes God feel close, personal, and attentive to the small things. But the question from the previous section still applies: is this confirming something God already said, or is meaning being assigned to something ordinary after the fact?

### 4. Dreams and Visions — Especially Secondhand Reports

Stories circulate, often shared widely online or passed along through friends and family, describing people in other countries — sometimes specifically Muslim-majority nations — having dreams or visions of Jesus that lead them to faith. These stories are often accepted and shared quickly, sometimes with excitement, as evidence of God moving in a specific, current, ongoing way.

It's worth being careful here in both directions. Scripture does record God communicating through dreams to specific people at specific moments tied to His larger plan — Joseph, Daniel, Cornelius in Acts 10, Paul in Acts 16. Those accounts are firsthand, specific, and tied to pivotal moments in the story of redemption. Whether God continues to communicate through dreams and visions today is a question Christians genuinely debate — Peter's citation of Joel 2 in Acts 2 is a passage continuationists appeal to legitimately, and that debate does not need to be resolved here. What is much easier to defend is this: regardless of one's position on that question, no serious biblical framework treats every dream or impression as reliable divine communication requiring no testing. "Test all things" (1 Thessalonians 5:21) applies equally in either direction. A secondhand story, repeated through several layers of retelling, that can't be verified, sits in a very different category — even if the underlying claim might be true.

### 5. Looking for Confirmation in Decisions

Asking God for a feeling of peace, an "open door," or a general "sense" before making a decision — and then treating whatever happens afterward as God's answer. This often comes with a hidden bias: the times it "worked" get remembered and retold, while the times the feeling was wrong, or nothing happened, or the door that seemed "open" led nowhere, tend to be quietly forgotten.

### 6. Noticing Patterns Because We're Looking for Them

This one is less spiritual and more just how minds work — but it's a big part of what's actually happening in a lot of these stories. If you start shopping for a particular car, you suddenly start seeing that car everywhere — not because there are more of them on the road, but because your attention is now tuned to notice it. The same thing happens with numbers, words, names, and symbols. Once something becomes meaningful to a person, their brain starts flagging every instance of it — and each new instance feels like confirmation, when really it's just attention doing what attention naturally does.

**Observation:** This isn't a criticism of anyone's intelligence or faith — it's simply how human attention works, and it's worth knowing about, because it explains a huge amount of what gets experienced as "too many coincidences to be chance."

### 7. Prophecy and "Signs of the Times" — A Different Category

Scripture does talk about signs in a different sense — specific, described events tied to the end times and God's redemptive plan (see passages like Matthew 24 and the book of Revelation). These are a separate category from the everyday personal "signs" discussed above. The danger is when the two get blended together — when the existence of real prophetic signs in Scripture gets used to justify treating ordinary daily life as a constant stream of personal omens. Scripture describing specific future events doesn't mean every unusual personal experience today is part of that same category.

## How Does God Actually Guide Us?

After spending this much time on what Scripture does not teach, a fair question is: if not signs, feelings, and personal omens, then how does God guide His people in the new covenant?

Scripture gives a clear and consistent answer — not through new ongoing personal revelation, but through means that are already in hand:

**The Word itself.** Psalm 119:105 — "Your word is a lamp to my feet and a light to my path." Scripture is not a static document awaiting supplementation by personal signs. It is living and active (Hebrews 4:12), sufficient to equip the believer for every good work (2 Timothy 3:16-17).

**Prayer and wisdom.** James 1:5 — "But if any of you lacks wisdom, let him ask of God, who gives to all generously and without reproach, and it will be given to him." The gift promised in response to asking is wisdom — the sanctified judgment to navigate circumstances — not a sign pointing to a predetermined answer.

**The counsel of others.** Proverbs 15:22 — "Without consultation, plans are frustrated, but with many counselors they succeed." The community of believers, grounded in the same Word, is itself a means of guidance.

**Providential circumstances, interpreted through Scripture.** God orders events — that is basic Christian doctrine. But the interpretive key for those events is always the Word, not our impressions about their meaning. The difference between providence and personal revelation is the difference between God ordering what happens and God encoding a personal message in it for us to decode. Scripture supports the first. It does not teach the second.

The goal is not a Christianity without guidance — it is a Christianity where guidance comes through the means God has already provided, tested against the standard He has already given.

---

## The Honest Middle Ground

God is sovereign over everything, including coincidences. Nothing happens outside His control. That part isn't in question, and it's held by essentially every Christian tradition.

But "God controls all things" is a different claim than "God is sending me a coded personal message through this specific event." The first is basic, historic Christian belief about God's providence. The second is a much bigger claim — and a bigger claim needs more than a feeling to support it.

The real danger isn't that someone might be wrong about a bird, a number, or a dream. The danger is the habit underneath it — training the mind to constantly search for hidden divine messages in random events. That habit is the same mental muscle behind horoscopes, fortune-telling, and omen-reading. Scripture doesn't tell us to feed that muscle. It tells us to starve it, and to anchor ourselves in what God has already, clearly said.

## A Closing Word — Not Just a Conclusion

This study didn't start as an abstract topic. It started with a conversation. Someone close to me shared something good — an eagle, flying low, maybe chasing something in the yard. A genuinely nice moment. But the conversation didn't stop there. It moved into stories about dreams — reports of people having visions of Jesus, especially in certain countries — and something about that didn't sit right with me. It felt like it was reaching for more than Scripture actually supports. That feeling is what sent me into this study in the first place.

So I started digging into what the Bible actually says about signs, dreams, and how God communicates. And partway through that study, while I was working through the very passages about sign-seeking, I stopped. Because I remembered something I'd said in that same conversation, before any of this started — something about the eagle. I'd made a comment that maybe this was God's way of showing us a heads-up about something coming. I said I was being facetious. But I'd said it.

And sitting there, studying the exact thing I was now questioning, it hit me: that's exactly what I just did. The dream stories made me uneasy enough to study this — but my own comment about the eagle was the same kind of thing. Smaller, maybe more casual, but the same instinct: looking at something ordinary and reaching for a hidden message in it.

While studying these passages, I remembered my own comment about the eagle — and Scripture exposed the same impulse in me. Whether the timing itself carried any special significance isn't the point, and it would be ironic to assign divine meaning to a moment in a study cautioning against exactly that. The Word of God did what Hebrews 4:12 says it does — it examined my own heart before I finished examining anyone else's.

> **Hebrews 4:12 (BSB)** *"For the word of God is living and active, and sharper than any two-edged sword, even penetrating as far as the division of soul and spirit, of both joints and marrow, and able to judge the thoughts and intentions of the heart."*

That's what it means when it says the Word of God is "living and active" — not just a text to study, but something that studies us back, able to judge "the thoughts and intentions of the heart." I went looking for an answer about someone else's situation, and the Word turned and asked a question about mine.
