# Baptism

## What It Is, What It Does, and How It Should Be Done

*Primary Translation: Berean Standard Bible (BSB)*

---

## I. The Word Itself

Every question about baptism eventually comes back to a single Greek verb, and what it actually meant before any tradition got hold of it.

The Greek word translated "baptize" throughout the New Testament is **βαπτίζω (*baptizō* (bap-TID-zoh))**. Its lexical meaning is to plunge, immerse, or submerge. In classical Greek it was used to describe dyeing cloth — plunging fabric into dye — and the sinking of ships. The word itself establishes full immersion as the original intent and the normative practice of the early church. The BSB renders the word consistently as "baptize" throughout — preserving the transliteration rather than the meaning, which is itself a translation choice worth knowing. The Greek is *baptizō* — to immerse or plunge — and the transliteration rather than translation of this word has shaped centuries of theological debate about mode.

This matters because the mode of baptism is not arbitrary. The act of going fully under the water and rising out of it carries the entire theological picture baptism is meant to represent. **The mode is the meaning.**

---

## II. Why Immersion

If the word itself points to immersion, the imagery of Scripture confirms it beyond doubt — because the picture baptism paints only works one way.

Full submersion is the preferred and intended mode of baptism because it embodies the death and resurrection of Jesus Christ — the very events that make baptism meaningful.

> **Romans 6:3-4 (BSB)** *"Or aren’t you aware that all of us who were baptized into Christ Jesus were baptized into His death? We were therefore buried with Him through baptism into death, in order that, just as Christ was raised from the dead through the glory of the Father, we too may walk in newness of life."*

Going under the water is burial with Christ. Coming up out of the water is resurrection with Christ. The imagery only works with full immersion. You cannot bury someone by sprinkling dirt on their head.

> **Acts 8:38-39 (BSB)** *"And he ordered the chariot to stop; and they both went down into the water, Philip as well as the eunuch, and he baptized him. When they came up out of the water, the Spirit of the Lord snatched Philip away."*

Down into the water. Up out of the water. This is the consistent physical picture of baptism in the NT narrative.

> **John 3:23 (BSB)** *"John also was baptizing in Aenon near Salim, because there was much water there; and people were coming and were being baptized."*

Much water was needed — consistent with immersion as the norm. Sprinkling requires no such provision.

## III. What Baptism Is

Baptism is not one thing. It is a single commanded act with multiple simultaneous realities — all of which require genuine faith to be operative, and none of which are produced by the water itself.

### 1. A Formal Appeal to God

> **1 Peter 3:21 (BSB)** *"Baptism now saves you — not the removal of dirt from the flesh, but an appeal to God for a good conscience — through the resurrection of Jesus Christ."*

The Greek word translated "appeal" is **ἐπερώτημα (*eperōtēma* (eh-peh-ROH-tay-mah))** — a formal pledge or commitment made in a covenantal context. Peter immediately qualifies his statement: it is not the removal of dirt from the flesh. The water has no inherent power. What saves is the appeal — the genuine faith commitment formally enacted — through the resurrection of Jesus Christ. Baptism is the appointed moment at which the inward faith of the believer is formally pledged before God.

### 2. The Washing Away of Sins

> **Acts 22:16 (BSB)** *"Now why do you delay? Get up and be baptized, and wash away your sins, calling on His name."*

The Greek word here is **ἀπολούω (*apolouō* (ah-poh-LOO-oh))** — to wash off or wash away thoroughly. This is distinct from **ἄφεσις (*aphesis* (AH-feh-sis))** — the releasing or forgiveness of the debt of sin that occurs at faith. Forgiveness cancels the debt. Baptism washes the person. These are two distinct transactions. Notice also the participle "calling on His name" — the washing is connected to genuine faith, not to the water acting independently.

### 3. Clothing Yourself with Christ

> **Galatians 3:26-27 (BSB)** *"For you are all sons of God through faith in Christ Jesus. For all of you who were baptized into Christ have clothed yourselves with Christ."*

Sonship — the saved relationship with God — is established in verse 26 through faith. Baptism in verse 27 describes what follows: clothing yourself with Christ. Putting Him on. The believer is now equipped and commissioned to walk as Christ calls. **Faith saves. Baptism clothes.**

### 4. Death and Resurrection with Christ

Romans 6:3-4 established the picture already: *"we have been buried with Him through baptism into death, so that as Christ was raised from the dead through the glory of the Father, so we too might walk in newness of life."* Paul is not making a salvation mechanics argument here — he is making a sanctification argument. Baptism is the marker of the old self dying and the new self rising. It launches the believer into newness of life. This is not metaphor — it is spiritual reality enacted through the appointed act of obedience.

## IV. What Baptism Is Not

Having established what baptism actually accomplishes, it is just as important to be precise about what it does not — because both errors are common, and both distort the gospel.

### 1. It Is Not the Mechanism of Salvation

Salvation is by grace through faith — full stop.

> **Ephesians 2:8-9 (BSB)** *"For by grace you have been saved through faith; and this is not of yourselves, it is the gift of God; not as a result of works, so that no one may boast."*

> **Galatians 3:26 (BSB)** *"For you are all sons of God through faith in Christ Jesus."*

> **Romans 10:9 (BSB)** *"that if you confess with your mouth, ‘Jesus is Lord,’ and believe in your heart that God raised Him from the dead, you will be saved."*

The debt — the wages of sin (Romans 6:23) — is cancelled at faith through the sacrifice of Christ. This is the forgiveness of the debt: *aphesis* — a releasing. Heaven is secured. The Holy Spirit indwells. None of this waits for baptism.

### 2. The Holy Spirit Is Not Withheld Until Baptism

> **Acts 10:44-48 (BSB)** *"While Peter was still speaking these words, the Holy Spirit fell upon all those who were listening to the message. And all the circumcised believers who came with Peter were amazed, because the gift of the Holy Spirit had been poured out on the Gentiles also. For they were hearing them speaking with tongues and exalting God. Then Peter answered, 'Surely no one can refuse the water for these to be baptized who have received the Holy Spirit just as we did, can he?' And he ordered them to be baptized in the name of Jesus Christ. Then they asked him to stay on for some days."*

Cornelius and his household received the Holy Spirit before baptism. Peter's response is telling — he recognizes the Spirit has already come and proceeds to baptize. This passage directly challenges any reading that makes baptism the moment of the Spirit's arrival.

### 3. It Is Not Merely a Symbol

Baptism is not simply an outward expression of an inward intention — that framing is too thin and is not found in Scripture. Baptism is a genuine multi-layered covenantal act in which real spiritual transactions occur: sins are washed away, Christ is put on, the old self dies and the new self rises. These are not performances of something already fully complete. They are real events made effectual by genuine faith and the power of Christ's resurrection.

### 4. It Is Not About the Water Itself

Peter states this explicitly in 1 Peter 3:21 — *"not the removal of dirt from the flesh."* The water has no inherent power. The mode matters because of what it represents, not because immersion itself accomplishes anything. What makes baptism operative is the genuine faith of the one being baptized, grounded in the resurrection of Jesus Christ.

## V. Key Passages Examined

Several passages speak directly to the relationship between baptism and salvation, and each one has been used at different points in church history to argue very different positions. Each deserves careful examination on its own terms.

### Acts 2:38

> *(BSB) "Repent and be baptized every one of you in the name of Jesus Christ for the forgiveness of your sins, and you will receive the gift of the Holy Spirit."*

The Greek preposition **εἰς (*eis* (ays))** — translated "for" — is genuinely flexible, and on its own this verse could be read either way: be baptized in order to obtain forgiveness, or be baptized in connection with forgiveness already granted through repentance and faith. Grammar alone does not settle this verse. What settles it is the rest of Scripture. Acts 10 records the Holy Spirit falling on Cornelius and his household before baptism — Peter responds by baptizing those who already have the Spirit. If receiving the Spirit, and with it the forgiveness and new birth it represents, required baptism first, this sequence could not happen. So whatever Peter means here, it is not a fixed, mechanical formula where nothing spiritual occurs until the water.

The better reading is that Peter is describing the whole conversion event as a single unit — repentance, forgiveness, baptism, and the Spirit, bound together as the normal pattern of becoming a Christian — not a strict checklist where step two unlocks step three. He is describing what happens together, using the language of *aphesis* (the releasing of the debt, accomplished through faith) alongside baptism (the washing, the *apolouō*, that follows it) because in the early church the two were never separated by more than a few hours.

### Mark 16:16

> *(BSB) "He who has believed and has been baptized shall be saved; but he who has disbelieved shall be condemned."*

*A textual note: Mark 16:9-20 is absent from the oldest and most reliable manuscripts, and most scholarly translations flag this section accordingly. That's worth knowing, but it doesn't need to settle the matter on its own.*

Even taking this verse at full face value, the structure itself is informative. The first half pairs belief and baptism: believed and has been baptized, shall be saved. But the condemnation clause that follows drops baptism entirely: he who has disbelieved shall be condemned — not "has not been baptized." If both were equally the dividing line between saved and condemned, the second half would need to name both. It names one.

**Belief is the hinge on which condemnation turns,** with baptism standing alongside it as the expected companion of genuine faith — not as a separately-judged condition.

### 1 Peter 3:21

Already quoted in Section III, Peter's statement bears revisiting in full: *"Baptism now saves you — not the removal of dirt from the flesh, but an appeal to God for a good conscience — through the resurrection of Jesus Christ."* Peter makes a strong claim, then immediately defines what he means by it. Baptism saves — but not because of the water itself. He explicitly rules that out. What saves is the appeal to God for a good conscience, made effectual through Christ's resurrection. The illustration Peter uses in the preceding verse makes this clear: the ark saved Noah's family, but not because the wood had power. The ark was the vessel God appointed; the water judged the world while those inside were carried safely through it.

Baptism works the same way — it is the appointed vessel of an appeal that only the resurrection of Christ can answer. The saving power is His, received through genuine faith; baptism is where that faith is formally pledged.

### Galatians 3:26-27

Already quoted in Section III: *"you are all sons of God through faith in Christ Jesus. For all of you who were baptized into Christ have clothed yourselves with Christ."* Read in sequence, these verses separate salvation and baptism as distinct realities. Sonship is through faith — verse 26. Clothing with Christ occurs at baptism — verse 27. The saved relationship precedes and produces the baptism that clothes the believer for new life.

## VI. How Baptism Should Be Administered

The New Testament consistently uses *baptizō* — to immerse — and the burial and resurrection imagery of Romans 6 makes full immersion the clear normative practice. The NT never explicitly states that immersion is the only valid mode, but the evidence consistently points to it as the original intent and practice.

The earliest post-apostolic document we possess — the Didache, or Teaching of the Twelve Apostles, dated AD 50-120 — addresses baptism directly and confirms this picture while also revealing the flexibility the early church exercised in practice.

> **Didache 7:1-3** *"Concerning baptism, baptize in this way. Having said all these things beforehand, baptize in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit in living water. But if you do not have living water, baptize in other water. And if you are not able to use cold water, use warm water. And if you do not have either, pour water three times on the head in the name of the Father and Son and Holy Spirit."*

Living water — **ὕδωρ ζῶν (*hudōr zōn* (HOO-dor zohn))** — means flowing water: a river, stream, or spring. This was the clear preference. The hierarchy of options — flowing water, still water, pouring as a last resort — confirms that full immersion was the norm. Pouring is offered only when immersion is not possible. The fact that an alternative is needed at all confirms immersion as the default. At the same time the Didache explicitly validates pouring under certain circumstances — written within living memory of the apostles. This directly challenges any position that declares immersion the only valid mode under all circumstances.

The conclusion the evidence supports is this: immersion is the clear and intended mode of baptism, rooted in the meaning of *baptizō*, confirmed by the burial and resurrection imagery of Scripture, and reflected in the practice of the earliest church. It should be the mode whenever possible. However Scripture never explicitly declares other modes invalid, and the Didache — our earliest practical church document — permits pouring when immersion is not available. The mode matters because of what it represents. What makes baptism valid is not the quantity of water but the genuineness of the faith of the one being baptized.

## VII. Baptism Is Commanded and Expected

Whatever theological distinctions are drawn, one conclusion is not in dispute: baptism is commanded throughout the New Testament and was practiced without delay in the early church. There is no category of unbaptized believer in the NT. Belief and baptism were one unified movement.

> **Matthew 28:19 (BSB)** *"Go therefore and make disciples of all the nations, baptizing them in the name of the Father and the Son and the Holy Spirit."*

> **Acts 2:38 (BSB)** *"Repent and be baptized every one of you in the name of Jesus Christ."*

> **Acts 8:36, 38 (BSB)** *"As they went along the road they came to some water; and the eunuch said, 'Look! Water! What prevents me from being baptized?' ... And he ordered the chariot to stop; and they both went down into the water, Philip as well as the eunuch, and he baptized him."*

*A textual note: verse 37, in which the eunuch makes a verbal confession of faith before baptism, is absent from the earliest and most reliable manuscripts and is omitted in most modern translations, including this one. The same caution applied to Mark 16:9-20 applies here.*

**The Ethiopian eunuch's response is the NT pattern — immediate.** No delay. No deferral. Genuine faith produces immediate obedience. The question "what prevents me?" is the right question for every believer.

Baptism is not optional for the genuine believer. It is the commanded, expected response to faith — the act in which the appeal is formally pledged, sins are washed away, Christ is put on, and death and resurrection with Him are enacted. To delay without cause or to dismiss it as unnecessary is to treat lightly what Scripture treats with weight.

---

## VIII. What Baptism Truly Is — A Summary

Baptism is not merely a symbol. It is not the mechanism of salvation. It is not about the water itself. It is not optional.

Baptism is a commanded, multi-layered covenantal act in which the believer formally pledges their appeal to God, is washed of their sins, is clothed with Christ, and enacts their death and resurrection with Him — all grounded in genuine faith, all made effectual by the resurrection of Jesus Christ.

Salvation — the canceling of the debt, the forgiveness of sins, the gift of the Holy Spirit, the securing of eternal life — is by grace through faith. Full stop. Baptism does not produce salvation. It does not cancel the debt. That was accomplished by Christ on the cross and received through genuine faith.

What baptism does is real and significant. It washes. It clothes. It buries and raises. It formally enacts before God what faith has already accomplished inwardly. It launches the believer into the fullest expression of the new life in Christ.

The preferred mode is full immersion — because going under the water and rising out of it is the burial and resurrection of Christ enacted in the body of the believer. This is why the earliest church practiced it this way, why the Didache prescribes it as the norm, and why the NT imagery consistently points to it. But it is the faith of the one being baptized — not the quantity of water — that makes baptism what God intends it to be.

---

*Believe. Repent. Be baptized. Walk in newness of life.*

This is the pattern of the New Testament. This is what the early church lived. This is what Scripture calls every believer to.

## IX. Ongoing Cleansing: Confession and the Stains of Sin

Baptism washes away sin and clothes the believer with Christ at the start of the new life — but it is a single event, not a repeatable one. The believer continues to live in a fallen body, in a world still marked by sin, and will continue to sin after baptism. Scripture is honest about this, and provides the mechanism for what happens next.

> **1 John 1:9 (BSB)** *"If we confess our sins, He is faithful and righteous to forgive us our sins and to cleanse us from all unrighteousness."*

Two words matter here. Forgive — **ἀφίημι (*aphiēmi* (ah-FEE-ay-mee))**, the same root as *aphesis* — the releasing of the debt. And cleanse — **καθαρίζω (*katharizō* (kah-thah-RID-zoh))** — a purifying word, the same family as the *apolouō* of baptism. Both realities baptism accomplished at the start — cancelling the debt, washing the person — remain available through confession. Not through repeated baptism, but through an ongoing posture of acknowledgment before God.

This is the mechanism for what happens after baptism. Confession is not a formality or a ritual phrase — it is the believer's continual turning back to God, naming what is true, and receiving both the forgiveness and the cleansing that flow from Christ's finished work.

Notice also who confession is made to. John does not say "to a priest" or require confession "to one another" as a precondition for forgiveness — he says "if we confess our sins," addressed directly to God, who is "faithful and righteous to forgive." No human intermediary stands between the believer and the Father here. Every believer, clothed with Christ at baptism, already has direct access — there is no priesthood of a select few standing in between. The mutual confession James 5:16 commands — confessing faults and praying for one another — is a separate practice aimed at accountability and healing among believers, not a sacramental requirement for receiving God's forgiveness. 1 John 1:9 is addressed to the individual believer and to God alone.

### What Happens Without It

Sin that goes unconfessed does not undo salvation. The debt remains cancelled — that was settled once, by faith, and is not revisited. But unconfessed sin does something else: it accumulates. It sits on the believer like a stain that was never washed out.

> **Ephesians 4:30 (BSB)** *"Do not grieve the Holy Spirit of God, by whom you were sealed for the day of redemption."*

The Spirit who indwells the believer at salvation is not driven out by sin — but He can be grieved. The word pictures a relationship under strain, not a relationship ended. A believer carrying unconfessed sin is not cut off from God, but their ability to hear, to discern, to walk in step with the Spirit is diminished. The stain does not change their standing before God; it changes their experience of walking with Him.

This is why Scripture so often pairs sin with imagery of darkness, heaviness, and distance — not because God has moved away, but because the believer's own posture has become harder to see through, harder to move freely in.

> **1 John 1:6-7 (BSB)** *"If we say that we have fellowship with Him and yet walk in the darkness, we lie and do not practice the truth; but if we walk in the Light as He Himself is in the Light, we have fellowship with one another, and the blood of Jesus His Son cleanses us from all sin."*

Fellowship — the closeness, the clarity, the active walking with God — is what is at stake here, not salvation itself. Walking in darkness while claiming fellowship is self-deception. Walking in the light — which includes the honesty of confession — is what keeps the relationship close and the cleansing ongoing.

### The Picture as a Whole

Salvation, by grace through faith, is settled once and does not need repeating. Baptism, the washing and clothing that follows, is also a single event — not repeated. But the believer's day-to-day walk requires an ongoing rhythm: sin, confession, forgiveness, cleansing, restored closeness. This is not a treadmill of guilt — it is the normal texture of a real relationship. A believer who confesses quickly and often stays close to God, walks in the light, and is not weighed down. A believer who lets sin go unaddressed accumulates distance — not from salvation, but from the nearness, clarity, and fruitfulness that Christ intends for those who walk with Him.

**This is why the call to confession is not a side issue.** It is the means by which the cleansing baptism began continues to do its work — daily, as needed, for as long as the believer walks in this life.
