# Who is The Angel of the Lord?

## A Study on the Malak Yahweh and the Pre-Incarnate Son

*Primary Translation: Berean Standard Bible (BSB)*

---

One of the most remarkable figures in the Old Testament is one most people read right past. He appears without introduction, speaks with the full authority of God, receives worship that no created being should receive, and is identified by the text and by those who encounter him as God Himself. He is called the Angel of the Lord — and the closer you look, the clearer it becomes that this is no ordinary messenger.

---

## The Hebrew Word Behind "Angel"

Before examining the passages, the word itself needs to be understood.

The Hebrew word translated "angel" throughout the Old Testament is **מַלְאָךְ (*malak* (mah-LAHK))**. It means, simply, messenger or sent one. It carries no inherent supernatural meaning. A human envoy sent by a king could be called a *malak*. A divine being sent by God could be called a *malak*. The word describes function — the role of one who is sent — not the nature of the one being sent.

This matters because "Angel of the Lord" in Hebrew is **מַלְאַךְ יְהוָה (*Malak Yahweh*)** — literally "Messenger of Yahweh." The title tells us He is sent. What the passages show us is who He actually is.

---

## What Distinguishes the Malak Yahweh

There are many messengers in the Old Testament — ordinary angels who deliver messages, carry out divine judgment, and minister to God's people. But the *Malak Yahweh* is consistently different from all of them in four specific ways:

**He speaks as God in the first person.** Not "thus says the Lord" — but "I will," "I am," "I have seen." He does not relay a message from God. He speaks as God.

**He receives worship without redirecting it.** When ordinary angels are worshipped in Scripture, they immediately correct it — "Do not do that; I am a fellow servant" (Revelation 22:8-9). The *Malak Yahweh* never does this. He accepts the homage.

**He is identified as God by the text itself.** In multiple accounts, the narrative begins with the *Malak Yahweh* appearing and ends with the text simply calling him God — no transition, no explanation, as if they are one and the same.

**Those who see him fear death for having seen God.** This is the consistent response of those who encounter him — not "I have seen a messenger" but "I have seen God and I will die" (Judges 13:22). No one in Scripture says this after encountering an ordinary angel.

This pattern, consistent across centuries and multiple authors of Scripture, points to one conclusion: the *Malak Yahweh* is not a created messenger. He is the second person of the Trinity — the eternal Son — appearing in visible, human-like form before the Incarnation.

## The Key Passages

### Genesis 16:7-13 — Hagar in the Wilderness

*Read the full passage in your Bible (Genesis 16:1-16). Key verses are quoted below.*

> **Genesis 16:7-8 (BSB)** *"The Angel of the LORD found Hagar near a spring in the desert; it was the spring that is beside the road to Shur. And he said, 'Hagar, slave of Sarai, where have you come from, and where are you going?'"*

> **Genesis 16:10-11 (BSB)** *"The Angel of the LORD also said to her: 'I will surely multiply your offspring so that they will be too numerous to count.' The Angel of the LORD continued: 'Behold, you are pregnant and will have a son. You shall name him Ishmael, for the LORD has heard your affliction.'"*

> **Genesis 16:13 (BSB)** *"She gave this name to the LORD who spoke to her: 'You are the God who sees me,' for she said, 'Have I truly seen the One who sees me?'"*

This is the first appearance of the *Malak Yahweh* in Scripture. Several things demand attention.

He promises to multiply Hagar's offspring — "I will multiply." That is not a messenger delivering God's promise. That is God making the promise Himself. Then in verse 13, Hagar does not say "I have spoken with God's messenger." She gives a name to the LORD who spoke to her — *El Roi*, the God who sees. She identifies the one she just spoke with as God Himself. The text affirms her. This is the pattern on its first occurrence: the *Malak Yahweh* appears, speaks as God, and is recognized as God.

---

### Genesis 22:11-18 — Abraham and Isaac on Mount Moriah

*Read the full passage in your Bible (Genesis 22:1-18). Key verses are quoted below.*

> **Genesis 22:11-12 (BSB)** *"But the Angel of the LORD called out to him from heaven: 'Abraham! Abraham!' 'Here I am,' he answered. 'Do not lay a hand on the boy,' he said. 'Do not do anything to him. Now I know that you fear God, because you have not withheld from me your son, your only son.'"*

> **Genesis 22:15-17 (BSB)** *"The Angel of the LORD called to Abraham from heaven a second time and said: 'By Myself I have sworn, declares the LORD, that because you have done this thing and have not withheld your son, your only son, I will surely bless you and will multiply your offspring...'"*

Notice verse 12: the *Malak Yahweh* says Abraham has not withheld his son *from Me* — speaking in the first person as the one to whom the sacrifice was being made. Then in verse 16, speaking again, he says "By Myself I have sworn, declares the LORD." The *Malak Yahweh* and the LORD are not two different persons being quoted in sequence. They are the same person speaking — which is exactly why the text moves between "the Angel" and "the LORD" without any sense of contradiction.

---

### Exodus 3:2-6 — Moses and the Burning Bush

> **Exodus 3:2-4 (BSB)** *"There the Angel of the LORD appeared to him in a blazing fire from within a bush. Moses saw that though the bush was on fire, it did not burn up. So Moses thought, 'I will go over and see this strange sight — why the bush does not burn up.' When the LORD saw that he had gone over to look, God called to him from within the bush, 'Moses! Moses!' And Moses said, 'Here I am.'"*

> **Exodus 3:6 (BSB)** *"Then He said, 'I am the God of your father, the God of Abraham, the God of Isaac and the God of Jacob.' At this, Moses hid his face, because he was afraid to look at God."*

This passage is a perfect example of the pattern. Verse 2: the *Malak Yahweh* appears in the fire. Verse 4: the LORD speaks from the fire. Verse 6: He identifies Himself as the God of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob — and Moses hides his face because he is afraid to look at God. There is no transition. No "and then God took over." The *Malak Yahweh* who appeared in the fire and the LORD who spoke from the fire are the same person. The text treats them as identical.

Jesus quotes this exact passage in the Gospels when the Sadducees challenge Him about the resurrection, saying God is the God of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob — the God of the living (Matthew 22:32). He is quoting from a passage about His own pre-incarnate appearance.

---

### Judges 6:11-23 — Gideon's Encounter

*Read the full passage in your Bible (Judges 6:11-24). Key verses are quoted below.*

> **Judges 6:12, 14 (BSB)** *"The Angel of the LORD appeared to Gideon and said, 'The LORD is with you, mighty warrior.'... The LORD turned to him and said, 'Go in the strength you have and save Israel out of Midian's hand. Am I not sending you?'"*

> **Judges 6:22-23 (BSB)** *"When Gideon realized that it was the Angel of the LORD, he exclaimed, 'Alas, Sovereign LORD! I have seen the Angel of the LORD face to face!' But the LORD said to him, 'Peace! Do not be afraid. You are not going to die.'"*

Again the pattern. The *Malak Yahweh* appears. The text moves seamlessly between calling him "the Angel of the LORD" and "the LORD" as if they are the same person — because they are. And Gideon's response upon realizing who he has spoken to is the same response every person has: he believes he will die because he has seen God face to face. An ordinary angel would never produce this response.

---

### Judges 13:3-22 — Manoah and the Birth of Samson

*Read the full passage in your Bible (Judges 13:1-24). Key verses are quoted below.*

> **Judges 13:3 (BSB)** *"The Angel of the LORD appeared to her and said, 'You are barren and childless, but you are going to become pregnant and give birth to a son.'"*

> **Judges 13:17-18 (BSB)** *"Then Manoah asked the Angel of the LORD, 'What is your name, so that we may honor you when your word comes true?' He replied, 'Why do you ask my name? It is beyond understanding.'"*

> **Judges 13:21-22 (BSB)** *"When the Angel of the LORD did not appear again to Manoah and his wife, Manoah realized it was the Angel of the LORD. 'We are doomed to die!' he said to his wife. 'We have seen God!'"*

When asked His name, the *Malak Yahweh* says it is "beyond understanding" — or in some translations "wonderful" (*pele* in Hebrew). This is the same Hebrew word used in Isaiah 9:6: "and His name shall be called Wonderful (*Pele*), Counselor, Mighty God, Everlasting Father, Prince of Peace." He declines to give His name because His name, fully understood, would answer the question completely. And again — Manoah concludes, as every person does, that he has seen God.

---

## The New Testament Anchor

Two passages in the New Testament establish the theological framework that explains everything above.

> **John 1:18 (BSB)** *"No one has ever seen God, but the one and only Son, who is Himself God and is at the Father's side, has made Him known."*

> **1 Timothy 6:16 (BSB)** *"who alone is immortal and who lives in unapproachable light, whom no one has seen or can see. To him be honor and might forever. Amen."*

The Father is never seen. He dwells in unapproachable light. No one has seen Him or can see Him. And yet the Old Testament is full of people seeing God — encountering Him visibly, physically, face to face.

The resolution is precise: every visible appearance of God in the Old Testament is the second person of the Trinity — the eternal Son, appearing in human-like form before the Incarnation. He is the one who makes the invisible God visible and knowable. This is exactly what John 1:18 says: no one has seen God, but the Son has made Him known. He has been making Him known since the beginning.

This is also why Jesus can say in John 8:58: *"Before Abraham was, I AM."* He was not merely aware of Abraham from a distance. He had appeared to him. He had spoken with him. He had eaten with him (Genesis 18). He was there.

---

## Why the Malak Yahweh Disappears

The *Malak Yahweh* is a prominent figure through the period of the Judges and into the early monarchy. Then, significantly, He becomes increasingly rare. After the prophetic period He essentially disappears — and there is no *Malak Yahweh* appearance in the New Testament.

The reason is the Incarnation.

The pre-incarnate Son had been appearing in temporary, visible form throughout the Old Testament to make the Father known and to accomplish specific redemptive purposes. With the Incarnation, that mode of appearance was no longer necessary — because the Son had now permanently taken on human flesh. He no longer appeared *like* a man temporarily. He had become a man permanently.

> **Hebrews 1:1-2 (BSB)** *"In the past God spoke to our ancestors through the prophets at many times and in various ways, but in these last days he has spoken to us by his Son, whom he appointed heir of all things, and through whom also he made the universe."*

The many ways God spoke — including through the *Malak Yahweh* — are now complete. In these last days He has spoken through His Son, who has come in flesh, died, risen, and ascended. The temporary appearances of the pre-incarnate Son gave way to the permanent reality of the Incarnate Son.

---

## Summary

The Angel of the Lord — the *Malak Yahweh* — is not a created messenger. He is the eternal Son of God, appearing in visible form throughout the Old Testament before taking on permanent human flesh in the Incarnation. This is confirmed by:

- The consistent pattern of Him speaking as God, receiving worship, and being identified as God
- The fact that those who encounter Him believe they have seen God and will die
- John 1:18 establishing that the Father is never seen — meaning every visible theophany is the Son
- 1 Timothy 6:16 confirming the Father dwells in unapproachable light no one has seen or can see
- Jesus' own statement in John 8:58 — "Before Abraham was, I AM" — claiming personal presence at events where the *Malak Yahweh* appeared

Every time you read "the Angel of the LORD" in your Old Testament, you are reading about the pre-incarnate Christ — present, active, and making the Father known long before Bethlehem.
