# Is There a Source of Truth?

## Manuscripts, Variants, and Why the Bible Can Still Be Trusted

*Primary Translation: Berean Standard Bible (BSB)*

---

## The Problem

Open two different English Bibles side by side and you'll eventually find a place where they don't say the same thing. One includes a verse the other omits. One says "Son of God," the other doesn't, in a given verse. For a lot of people, that discovery is unsettling — if the Bible has variations across translations, how can anyone trust that any of it is the actual, reliable word of God?

It's worth being honest about something before we go any further: we do not possess a single original manuscript of any book of the Bible. Not one autograph, penned by Paul's or John's own hand, has survived. What we have instead are copies — thousands of them, made by hand, across centuries, in different regions, by different scribes. That's not a modern discovery meant to shake your faith. It's simply the historical reality, and it's a reality shaped in part by deliberate, violent effort.

In February of 303 AD, the Roman emperor Diocletian ordered the newly built Christian church at Nicomedia razed, its scriptures burned, and its treasures seized — the opening act of what became the most severe, systematic persecution of Christians in Roman history. The edict that followed prohibited Christians from gathering for worship and ordered the destruction of their scriptures, liturgical books, and places of worship across the entire empire. Christians were compelled to hand over their sacred texts to be burned; those who refused were imprisoned, tortured, or killed. It was, by design, an attempt to erase the Bible and the faith built on it entirely. It failed. Diocletian himself abdicated in 305 AD — one of the only Roman emperors in history to leave office alive and by his own choice, rather than by assassination, disease, or battlefield defeat. The faith he tried to erase outlived him, outlived the empire that persecuted it, and became that empire's own religion within a decade of his death.

So the question isn't really "why don't we have a perfect original copy." Empires have tried to make sure of that. The real question is: given that reality, can we still trust what we have?

## Three Families, Not One Fight

Nearly every disagreement about the biblical text traces back to which underlying Greek manuscripts a translation is built on. There are three main groups worth knowing, because they get confused with each other constantly — including, in the past, by us.

**The Byzantine text, or Majority Text.** This is the text type represented by the overwhelming majority of surviving Greek manuscripts — historically centered on the region around Antioch and later Constantinople, the heart of where the early church actually operated and where most New Testament books were originally sent.

**The Textus Receptus.** This is a specific 16th-century printed Greek text, compiled by Erasmus, that underlies the King James and New King James Versions. Here's the correction worth making plainly: the Textus Receptus is not the same thing as the Majority Text, even though the two get treated as interchangeable constantly. Erasmus worked from a small handful of manuscripts — in some places fewer than a dozen — and patched gaps by translating back from the Latin Vulgate. As a result, the Textus Receptus actually differs from the broader Byzantine Majority Text in close to 2,000 places. Someone can hold to Majority Text priority without holding to Textus Receptus priority, or KJV-Only positions. They are related, but they are not the same claim.

**The Critical Text.** This is the modern eclectic Greek text underlying most contemporary translations, built primarily on the earliest surviving manuscripts — chiefly Codex Sinaiticus and Codex Vaticanus, both fourth-century, both traced to Alexandria, Egypt.

## The Evidence Itself

Whatever position anyone lands on, the sheer scale of the evidence is not in dispute. There are more than 5,000 surviving Greek New Testament manuscripts — a body of textual evidence with no parallel anywhere in the ancient world. The overwhelming majority of these, roughly 90 percent, reflect the Byzantine text form.

Put that in perspective. Enormous amounts of what's taught as settled ancient history — events, speeches, entire reigns — rest on a small handful of surviving documents, sometimes just one, written well after the fact by a single author. That single source is routinely treated as reliable history. The New Testament, by contrast, survives in thousands of independent manuscript witnesses, copied across multiple regions and centuries, that overwhelmingly corroborate the same content. If a single ancient source is treated as good enough to establish history elsewhere, a manuscript tradition backed by thousands of independent, cross-corroborating witnesses deserves more confidence than that single source — not more suspicion.

## The Variants, Honestly Counted

So what about the places where the manuscripts actually disagree? This is where the fight usually loses its heat once you look closely at the numbers.

The overwhelming majority of all textual variants across the entire manuscript tradition are things like spelling differences, word order, or the presence or absence of a movable letter at the end of a Greek word — differences invisible in translation and irrelevant to meaning. When every category is added up, well over 99 percent of all textual variants have no effect on the meaning of the text whatsoever.

What remains is a genuinely small list of notable places where manuscripts differ in a way that affects the reading — the Comma Johanneum in 1 John 5:7-8 (an explicit Trinitarian formula found only in a handful of late manuscripts), the ending of Mark's Gospel after 16:8, and the story of the woman caught in adultery in John 7:53-8:11. These are worth knowing about, and good study Bibles typically flag them with a footnote. But even here, the core teaching involved — the Trinity, the resurrection, grace toward sinners — is affirmed clearly and repeatedly elsewhere in passages no one disputes. No textual variant anywhere in the manuscript tradition puts a single core doctrine of the faith at risk. That claim isn't ours alone — it's close to a consensus statement among textual critics across the theological spectrum, whatever position they hold on which text family is best.

## The Geography Argument

One more piece worth understanding, because it's the strongest case Majority Text advocates make, and it deserves to be stated fairly.

Codex Sinaiticus and Codex Vaticanus are older than almost anything in the Byzantine manuscript tradition — both dating to the fourth century. Age is real evidence, and it's the main reason modern scholarship leans toward the Alexandrian text type: older generally means fewer generations of copying between it and the original.

But age isn't the whole story, and here's the honest counter-argument. Egypt's dry climate preserved ancient documents in a way almost nowhere else in the ancient world could. Manuscripts used and copied in Asia Minor and Greece — the actual heartland of the early church, where nearly all of the New Testament books were originally sent — simply didn't survive the climate the way Egyptian papyri did. So the oldest surviving copies being Alexandrian may reflect what happened to survive, not necessarily what was most original or most widely used. Majority Text advocates argue that a reading attested consistently across thousands of manuscripts, copied for centuries in the very region the New Testament was written to and used by, is stronger evidence of authenticity than two very old manuscripts from a single, geographically distinct location — however historically and intellectually significant Alexandria was in its own right (and it was; it was home to one of the ancient world's great centers of learning and a major early Christian theological school).

Both arguments are reasonable. Both are made by serious, faithful scholars. Neither one, as we've already shown, changes a single core doctrine either way.

## Where I Landed

For a long time, I considered myself a Majority Text person — and having walked through the logic above, that position still holds together. The geography, the sheer weight of manuscript evidence, the fact that the Byzantine text represents the region the New Testament was actually written to: it's a coherent, defensible case, and I can explain why someone holds it.

But the more I studied this — applying the same standard we try to hold to throughout this site, testing everything against Scripture and evidence rather than assuming a position because it's familiar or comfortable — the more I realized the disagreement itself isn't where the weight of the argument should sit. I used to think which text family was "right" mattered a great deal. I don't think that anymore. What matters is what we've already shown: no matter which manuscript family you trust, the same essential story, the same core doctrines, survive fully intact.

## Where This Leaves Us

The copies we have are not the original autographs. They were made by fallible men, across centuries, and they contain real differences — spelling, word order, and a small handful of disputed passages. That's not evidence against Scripture. It's simply what happens when a text is copied by hand for two thousand years by people doing their honest best, without malice, occasionally making mistakes the way any of us would.

What Scripture's reliability never actually depended on was having one single perfect original copy sitting in a vault somewhere. It depended on the word of God being preserved through the evidence — corroborated, cross-checked, and multiplied across more manuscripts, more regions, and more centuries than any other ancient document in existence. An empire tried to burn every copy of this book off the face of the earth and failed within a decade. Copyists across a thousand years of hand transmission, working independently across three continents, kept landing on the same story. That is not the profile of a book that got lost or corrupted. That's the profile of a book God intended to survive — and did.
