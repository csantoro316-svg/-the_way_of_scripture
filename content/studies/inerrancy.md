# Is the Bible the Inerrant Word of God?

## A Study on Inspiration, Manuscripts, and Translation

*Primary Translation: Berean Standard Bible (BSB)*

---

## Purpose

Yes — the Bible is the inspired, inerrant Word of God. But that statement requires a critical clarification that most churches never make: inerrancy belongs to the original Hebrew and Greek texts as God inspired them. Every English translation is a human interpretive act. Some translations carry demonstrable theological and political bias introduced by the very men who produced them. This document examines what inerrancy actually means, the manuscript evidence that supports it, and the documented history of how translation choices have shaped — and in some cases distorted — what ordinary readers encounter when they open their Bibles.

---

## The Pattern Starts in the Garden

Man has been altering God's Word since the beginning. This is not a modern problem. It did not start with King James, or with the Roman Catholic Church, or with Constantine. It started in Genesis 3.

> **Genesis 2:17 (BSB)** *"But you must not eat from the tree of the knowledge of good and evil; for in the day you eat of it, you will surely die."*

When Eve recounts this command to the serpent in Genesis 3, something has already shifted:

> **Genesis 3:2-3 (BSB)** *"We may eat the fruit of the trees of the garden, but about the fruit of the tree in the middle of the garden, God has said, 'You must not eat of it or touch it, or you will die.'"*

Eve added the words "or touch it." God never said that. And then the serpent built directly on that mishandling:

> **Genesis 3:4 (BSB)** *"You will not surely die," the serpent told her.*

First, addition. Then, contradiction. The plain statement of God was first expanded beyond what He said, then directly inverted. The pattern was established at the very first interaction between man and the Word of God: man modifies, distorts, and adds to what God said.

The Apostle John would later warn that anyone who adds to or takes away from the words of Scripture faces severe consequence:

> **Revelation 22:18-19 (BSB)** *"I testify to everyone who hears the words of prophecy in this book: If anyone adds to them, God will add to him the plagues described in this book. And if anyone removes words from this book of prophecy, God will remove his share in the tree of life and in the holy city, which are described in this book."*

This is not a footnote in Scripture — it is a recurring theme from Genesis to Revelation. Every documented case of translation bias explored in this study is part of that same ancient pattern. Knowing that should make us neither cynical nor defeated. It should make us more determined to go back to what God actually said.

## What Inerrancy Actually Means

Two passages establish the foundation for the doctrine of inspiration:

> **2 Timothy 3:16 (BSB)** *"All Scripture is God-breathed and is useful for instruction, for conviction, for correction, and for training in righteousness."*

> **2 Peter 1:20-21 (BSB)** *"Above all, you must understand that no prophecy of Scripture comes from one's own interpretation. For no such prophecy was ever brought forth by the will of man, but men spoke from God as they were carried along by the Holy Spirit."*

Notice what these passages claim. The claim in 2 Timothy 3:16 is about origin — God breathed out the Scriptures. The claim in 2 Peter 1:21 is about the mechanism — the Holy Spirit carried men along as they wrote. Neither passage makes a claim about English translations produced 1,500 to 2,000 years later by committees working under political, institutional, and theological pressures.

Inerrancy applies to the **autographs** — the original manuscripts as God inspired them. This does not leave us without confidence. It means we must be honest about what we are holding in our hands when we pick up any English Bible: a translation, not the autograph. A faithful representation in most respects — but a human work nonetheless, subject to the limitations and, in some cases, the agendas of those who produced it.

## The Manuscript Evidence

We do not have the original manuscripts. What we do have is an extraordinary wealth of copies that allows us to reconstruct the original text with remarkable confidence. The numbers speak for themselves.

### New Testament Manuscript Count

Approximately 5,800 Greek manuscripts of the New Testament are known to exist. To put that in historical perspective:

- **Homer's Iliad** — roughly 1,800 manuscripts. Considered one of the most well-attested works of antiquity.
- **Julius Caesar's Gallic Wars** — approximately 250 manuscripts.
- **Plato's writings** — roughly 7 manuscripts, the earliest copy made 1,200 years after Plato wrote.
- **Tacitus' Annals** — approximately 20 manuscripts, the earliest copy roughly 1,000 years after the original.

No one in serious academic circles questions the reliability of Plato, Tacitus, or Caesar based on manuscript count. Yet the New Testament, with 5,800 manuscripts, faces scrutiny those works never do. The standard of evidence applied to the Bible is one applied to no other ancient document. By any honest historical measure, the New Testament manuscript tradition is not just reliable — it is unprecedented.

### The Manuscript Families

There are two primary manuscript traditions that scholars work from:

**Majority Text / Byzantine Tradition:** Thousands of manuscripts, later in date, with remarkable internal consistency among themselves.

**Codex Sinaiticus and Codex Vaticanus:** Older in date, fewer in number, discovered under different circumstances, and used heavily by modern critical text scholars.

There is a genuine and ongoing scholarly debate about which tradition is more reliable. That debate will be addressed in depth in a dedicated companion document. For the purposes of this study, the critical point is this: across all manuscript families, on every doctrinally significant passage — the nature of Christ, the resurrection, salvation, the character of God — the manuscripts agree. The variations that exist between families are largely minor: differences in word order, spelling, and occasional phrase variation. Nothing that alters core doctrine. Nothing that changes what God requires of us or what He has done for us in Christ.

**The manuscript evidence does not undermine inerrancy. It confirms that what we are reading faithfully represents what was written.**

## The King James Version and the Translation Directives

The King James Bible, published in 1611, remains the most widely distributed English Bible in history. It is a literary achievement, and the scholars who produced it were largely learned and serious men. That acknowledgment matters — because what follows is not an attack on those men. It is an examination of the constraints placed upon them by royal decree, and the consequences those constraints produced.

When King James commissioned the translation in 1604, he issued 15 specific rules that his translators were required to follow before they opened a single Greek manuscript. Two of those rules had direct and lasting theological consequences.

### Rule 3: The *Ekklēsia* Decision

Rule 3 stated: *"The Old Ecclesiastical Words to be kept, viz. the word Church not to be translated Congregation &c."*

In plain terms: the translators were forbidden from rendering the Greek word **ἐκκλησία (*ekklēsia* (ek-klay-SEE-ah))** as "congregation" or "assembly" — its accurate meaning — and were required to use "church" instead, regardless of what the Greek said.

This was not a linguistic decision. It was a political one. King James was the head of the Church of England. If *ekklēsia* meant "called-out assembly of people" — which it does — then his institutional authority had no biblical foundation. A called-out assembly of people has no need for a king at its head. So the word became "church": a building, an institution, a hierarchy that James sat atop.

### What Every Bible Before the KJV Actually Said

Prior to the KJV, every Greek-to-English New Testament translation rendered *ekklēsia* as "congregation" or "assembly":

- **William Tyndale's New Testament (1524)** — "congregation"
- **The Coverdale Bible (1535)** — "congregation"
- **The Matthew Bible (1537)** — "congregation"
- **The Great Bible (1539)** — "congregation"

The only English Bible before the KJV that used "church" was the 1382 Wycliffe Bible — and that translation was made not from the Greek, but from the Latin Catholic Vulgate, which carried its own institutional bias.

Every translator who worked from the actual Greek text said the same thing: the word means assembly. The congregation. The called-out people. Not a building. Not an institution. The people themselves.

### William Tyndale: Executed for Getting It Right

William Tyndale was one of the first men to translate the New Testament into English from the original Greek. He rendered *ekklēsia* as "congregation" — because that is what the Greek says. His translation would have been read by ordinary people and would have exposed the institutional church's claim to biblical authority as having no textual foundation.

The religious establishment could not allow that. Tyndale was hunted, betrayed, arrested, and in 1536 was strangled and burned at the stake.

**A man was executed specifically to prevent the accurate translation of one Greek word from reaching ordinary people. Let that land.**

Note: The BSB, like most modern English translations, renders *ekklēsia* as "church" throughout. This site's content on the church examines this translation choice directly — the argument is made from the Greek, not from the translation.

Decades later, King James codified that same inaccuracy into law by royal decree — and it has been repeated in English Bibles ever since. The BSB, like every major modern English translation, continues to render *ekklēsia* as "church." This is not a failure of the BSB specifically — it reflects a convention so deeply embedded in English Bible translation that no single translation has reversed it. The argument must be made from the Greek, which is why this site always goes to the original word.

### The Etymology of "Church"

There is one more layer to this that most people never hear. The English word "church" is not actually a translation of *ekklēsia* at all. It derives from an entirely different Greek word — **κυριακόν (*kuriakon* (koo-ree-ah-KON))** — meaning "belonging to the Lord" or "the Lord's house." *Kuriakon* appears only twice in the entire New Testament, and neither occurrence refers to what we call "the church" today.

So the word "church" does not just mistranslate *ekklēsia* — it imports a completely foreign concept into the text. A concept of a place, a house, a building. *Ekklēsia* is people. *Kuriakon* is a house. They are not the same word, they do not carry the same meaning, and one was deliberately substituted for the other.

## The Pattern Continues

The *ekklēsia* decision is the most politically documented example of translation bias in the English Bible tradition — but it is not the only one. The same pattern of translation choices shaping doctrine, sometimes distorting it beyond recognition, runs through the New Testament on multiple significant terms.

The Greek word **προσκυνέω (*proskuneō* (PROS-koo-neh-oh))**, which means to bow down or prostrate oneself in homage, is rendered generically as "worship" — collapsing a specific, embodied act of submission into a vague modern concept of religious feeling. The word **λατρεύω (*latreuō* (lat-REW-oh))**, meaning priestly service or sacred vocation, is similarly flattened to "worship" — erasing the distinction between a lifestyle of holy service and an hour on Sunday morning. The word **ἀσθενέω (*asthenoō* (as-theh-NEH-oh))**, meaning "weak," is rendered "sick" in James 5:14 — a choice that generated an entire theology of miraculous physical healing from a passage that may have been addressing spiritual restoration.

That document represents hours of study into the original Greek terms behind the English word "worship" — and what their recovery reveals about what the early Church actually practiced and believed.

## Key Takeaways

- Inerrancy belongs to the original Hebrew and Greek autographs — not to any English translation.
- The manuscript evidence for the New Testament is stronger than for any other ancient document in history. Core doctrine is consistent across all manuscript families.
- King James issued 15 translation directives that his scholars were required to follow regardless of what the Greek said. Rule 3 forbade the accurate translation of *ekklēsia*.
- Every Greek-to-English translator before the KJV rendered *ekklēsia* as "congregation" or "assembly." William Tyndale was executed in 1536 for doing so.
- The English word "church" does not derive from *ekklēsia* at all. It derives from *kuriakon* — a different Greek word meaning "the Lord's house." A word for a place was substituted for a word for a people.
- This pattern of translation choices shaping and distorting doctrine did not begin with King James and did not end with him. It began in the Garden and continues today.
- The answer is not to distrust Scripture. The answer is to go deeper — back to what God actually said, in the language He said it.
