# The Way of Scripture

## Welcome

The name of this site comes from the earliest followers of Jesus, who were not called Christians — they were called followers of **The Way** (Acts 9:2, 19:9, 24:14). That name was not incidental. It described people who had found not just a belief system, but a path — one rooted in the person of Christ, walked out in daily life, and grounded in what God had actually said.

That is what this site is for.

---

## A Note on Our Translation Choice

Every document on this site quotes Scripture from the **Berean Standard Bible (BSB)**.

The Berean Standard Bible was chosen for three reasons. First, it is freely available for ministry use — consistent with this site's commitment to making all reference content free to everyone. Second, it is a modern, generally literal translation designed to stay close to the original Hebrew and Greek while remaining readable in English. Third, its name reflects the Acts 17:11 standard this site is built on: the Bereans who examined the Scriptures daily to see whether these things were so.

## The Problem Is Not the Word

The Bible — the collected books of the Hebrew and Greek Scriptures — is the inerrant Word of God. That is the foundation everything here is built on. Not the inerrant Word of God as filtered through a particular translation, a particular denomination, or a particular era of church history. The original Hebrew and Greek texts: those are what God inspired, and those are what this site goes back to.

The problem is not the Word. The problem is what men have done to it.

That problem is older than you might think. It begins in Genesis 3.

---

## It Started in the Garden

God's instruction to Adam was plain:

> **Genesis 2:17 (BSB)** *"but you must not eat from the tree of the knowledge of good and evil; for in the day that you eat of it, you will surely die.”*

By the time Eve encounters the serpent in Genesis 3, something has already shifted. When the serpent asks what God said, Eve responds:

> **Genesis 3:3** *"from the fruit of the tree which is in the middle of the garden, God has said, 'You shall not eat from it or touch it, lest you die.'"*

God never said anything about touching it. Eve added to the Word. She did not intend harm. She was not conspiring against God. But she mishandled what He had said — and the serpent built directly on that mishandling:

> **Genesis 3:4** *"You surely will not die!"*

First, addition. Then, contradiction. The plain statement of God — *"you will surely die"* — was first expanded beyond what God said, then directly inverted.

Scripture takes this pattern so seriously that it bookends the entire Bible with the same warning:

> **Deuteronomy 4:2 (BSB)** *"You must not add to or subtract from what I command you, so that you may keep the commandments of the LORD your God that I am giving you."*

> **Revelation 22:18-19 (BSB)** *"I testify to everyone who hears the words of the prophecy of this book: if anyone adds to them, God will add to him the plagues which are written in this book; and if anyone takes away from the words of the book of this prophecy, God will take away his part from the tree of life."*

The warning at the beginning and the warning at the end are the same warning. Do not add. Do not subtract. Do not alter what God said.

## What Happened to the Word

The history of the Bible in translation is, in many places, a history of that same pattern playing out — not through malice in most cases, but through institutional pressure, cultural accommodation, and the quiet power of those who controlled what ordinary people were allowed to read.

One of the clearest examples is the Greek word **ἐκκλησία (*ekklēsia* (ek-klay-SEE-ah))** — the word the New Testament consistently uses for the gathered people of God. Its meaning is straightforward: a called-out assembly of people. Not a building. Not an institution. The people themselves.

When King James commissioned the English Bible that would bear his name in 1611, his translators were given fifteen rules to follow. Rule 3 directed that *ekklēsia* was not to be translated as "congregation" or "assembly" — its actual meaning — but as **"church."** The word *church* derives from the Greek *kuriakon*, meaning "belonging to the Lord" — a word applied to buildings, not people. The two words are not the same. They do not mean the same thing. And the choice to substitute one for the other was not a translation decision. It was a political one, made to preserve the existing structure of the Church of England.

That single substitution, carried forward into virtually every major English translation that followed, helped cement a model of Christianity built around institutions and buildings — one that the New Testament, read in its original Greek, simply does not teach.

This is not an isolated example. It is a pattern.

## Reading the Bible Is Not as Simple as Reading the Bible

Beyond what happened in translation, there is a layer most people never think about — and it may be the most important one of all.

Scripture is not always easy to read correctly on its own. That is not a criticism of any reader. It is simply true, and the Bible itself acknowledges it. A single verse pulled from the middle of a passage can appear to mean the exact opposite of what the full passage teaches. A promise made to a specific people in a specific historical moment gets lifted out of that context and applied universally — and the meaning collapses. A Greek word with a precise, narrow meaning gets rendered by a broad English word that flattens the distinction the original was drawing. These things happen constantly, and most readers have no way of knowing.

Context matters. Who is being spoken to, when, and why — these things change what a passage means. The historical world behind the text matters. The original language matters. And the relationship between any given passage and the rest of Scripture matters enormously — because Scripture interprets Scripture, and a verse read in isolation from everything around it is a verse already halfway misread.

But here is the deeper problem. Most people do not form their own understanding of Scripture by reading it. They receive someone else's interpretation — from a pastor, a teacher, a tradition, a commentary, a social media post — and they accept it. Not out of laziness, but because that is how the system works. You sit in a pew. Someone with credentials tells you what the text means. You receive it, repeat it, and pass it on. And somewhere in that chain, what the text actually says and what people believe it says quietly diverge — and no one notices, because no one is going back to check.

This is how it perpetuates.

And it is not a new problem. The believers in Berea understood it two thousand years ago. When Paul himself came to teach them, they did not simply defer to his authority:

> **Acts 17:11 (BSB)** *"Now the Bereans were more noble-minded than the Thessalonians, for they received the message with great eagerness and examined the Scriptures every day to see if these teachings were true."*

They tested what even an apostle said against Scripture itself. That is the standard Scripture holds up as noble — not deference to authority, not trust in tradition, but personal, daily examination of the text. It is the standard this site holds itself to as well. Nothing here is above that examination. Everything here is an invitation to go and check.

## What We Believe

We believe the core gospel message is preserved across all major modern translations. The deity of Christ, His death and resurrection, salvation by grace through faith — these truths are not in dispute. Every major translation agrees on the foundation.

What we are examining here are the downstream effects of specific translation choices on specific doctrines — places where the original Greek or Hebrew says something measurably different from what the English renders, and where those differences have shaped what millions of people believe the Bible teaches. Translation choices are not neutral. They carry theological weight. And when those choices are made under institutional pressure, or when context is stripped away in the teaching of a passage, or when a single verse is handed to someone without the surrounding text that gives it meaning — the result is a Christianity shaped more by tradition and assumption than by what Scripture actually says.

We are not anti-translation. We are not anti-church. We are not here to tear down. We are pro-accuracy. The goal is not to undermine confidence in Scripture — it is to deepen it, by going back to what Scripture actually says, in the language it was written in, in the context it was written for, before centuries of tradition shaped what we were handed.

## What This Site Is

This site is a reference library, built document by document, passage by passage, word by word. Every major topic — baptism, prayer, worship, the church, faith, discipleship — is examined through the original languages, with the earliest sources and the best available scholarship brought to bear on what the text actually says.

Nothing here is claimed as the final word. Every document is a working document, open to correction by Scripture itself. The standard is the Berean standard — examine it, test it, and go back to the text. If something here is wrong, Scripture will show it. That is the only authority this site answers to.

## Why It Matters

Most people inherit their theology. They receive it from the tradition they were raised in, the church they attend, the translation they were handed as a child. None of that is wrong in itself. But it means that most people have never actually read what the Bible says — they have read what their tradition says the Bible says, filtered through translation choices they were never told about, shaped by historical forces they were never shown, and passed on by teachers who themselves received it the same way.

The gap between those two things is real. It matters for how people understand salvation, baptism, prayer, worship, the church — the most fundamental things. And it closes the same way it always has: by going back to the Word itself, reading it in full, examining it in context, and letting it say what it actually says.

Not with anger. Not with a new denomination or a new tradition to replace the old ones. With the text itself — returned to its original language, examined in its original context, and allowed to speak.

*"Your word is a lamp to my feet and a light to my path."* — Psalm 119:105
