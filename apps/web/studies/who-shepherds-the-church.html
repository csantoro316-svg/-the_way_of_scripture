Who Shepherds the Church?

Elders, Overseers, Shepherds, and the Shape of Leadership in the Church

Primary Translation: Berean Standard Bible (BSB)

Walk into most churches on a Sunday and the structure tells you everything before a word is said. One man on a stage. A title beneath his name on the sign outside. A congregation seated, facing forward, listening. Somewhere along the way, "elder" became "Pastor," "overseer" became "Bishop," and a room full of believers gathered to wrestle with Scripture together became an audience.

We've already made the case elsewhere for what the church actually is at its core — see The True Church According to Jesus for that fuller argument. Here, the question is narrower and more practical: who leads it, and how? Did the New Testament establish titled offices and a chain of command, or something else entirely?

Titles or Roles?

The New Testament uses several words to describe those who lead a local body: presbyteros (elder), episkopos (overseer/bishop), diakonos (servant/deacon), and poimen (shepherd/pastor). The modern church has stacked these into a hierarchy — deacon below elder, elder below bishop, bishop ruling over a diocese. But that's not how the New Testament uses them.

Look at how Paul uses the words in the same breath, about the same men:

"For this reason I left you in Crete, that you would set in order what remains and appoint elders (presbyterous) in every city as I directed you... For the overseer (episkopon) must be above reproach as God's steward." (Titus 1:5, 7)

Elder and overseer are the same office, not two rungs on a ladder. Paul does it again more explicitly in Acts:

"Now from Miletus he sent to Ephesus and called for the elders (presbyterous) of the church... Watch out for yourselves and for all the flock, among whom the Holy Spirit has made you overseers (episkopous), to shepherd (poimainein) the church of God." (Acts 20:17, 28)

One group of men. Called elders. Made overseers. Told to shepherd. Three words, one office, one function — caring for people, not ruling a rank. Peter makes the same connection:

"So I exhort the elders (presbyterous) among you... shepherd (poimanate) the flock of God among you, exercising oversight (episkopountes)..." (1 Peter 5:1-2)

Elder, shepherd, overseer — again the same men, the same task, described three ways. Not a hierarchy. A job, seen from three angles: age and maturity (elder), responsibility for watching over souls (overseer), and the actual daily work of tending people (shepherd).

And notice something else in every one of these passages: the word is always plural. Elders. Overseers. Never a solitary bishop over a region. Every church Paul planted got a plurality of men recognized for this work — not one man elevated above the rest.

Diakonos (deacon/servant) is different in kind, not rank — a recognized role of practical service (see 1 Timothy 3:8-13, Acts 6:1-6), never presented as a rung beneath elder in a chain of command, but as its own distinct function.

None of these words appears in the New Testament used the way we use "Pastor" or "Bishop" today — as a formal title of address, a name someone is called by, a status held for life. Peter is never called "Bishop Peter." Paul never addresses a letter to "Bishop Timothy" or "Bishop Titus," even though both men were doing overseer-level work. They're addressed by name. Their function is described. The function never swallows the name.

(For how the early church gathered and what happened when it did, see Worship as the Early Church Knew It for the fuller picture — the leadership question and the worship question are two sides of the same recovery.)

What Changed at the Cross

There's a deeper reason a formal priestly class has no place in the church, and it's not just a pattern we notice in the text — it's an argument the text makes explicitly.

The book of Hebrews spends its middle chapters establishing that Jesus is a priest "according to the order of Melchizedek" (Hebrews 5:6, quoting Psalm 110:4) rather than according to the order of Levi. Melchizedek appears once in Genesis, blessing Abraham — a king and priest with no recorded genealogy, no predecessor, no successor (Genesis 14:18-20; see also Hebrews 7:1-3). The writer of Hebrews uses that silence deliberately: a priesthood that isn't passed down, isn't inherited, isn't mediated through a class of men set apart by birth.

Then comes the argument that matters most for this study:

"Now if perfection had been attainable through the Levitical priesthood... what further need would there have been for another priest to arise according to the order of Melchizedek, rather than one named after the order of Aaron? For when the priesthood is changed, of necessity there is a change in the law as well." (Hebrews 7:11-12)

A change in priesthood. A change in law. The old covenant required a mediating class — men set apart, functioning between God and the people, offering sacrifices the people themselves could not offer. Hebrews' whole argument is that this pointed forward to something, and that something has already arrived:

"Consequently he is able to save to the uttermost those who draw near to God through him, since he always lives to make intercession for them... he holds his priesthood permanently, because he continues forever." (Hebrews 7:25, 24)

One priest. Permanent. No successor needed, because he never dies. And the practical consequence for every believer:

"Therefore, brothers, since we have confidence to enter the holy places by the blood of Jesus, by the new and living way that he opened for us..." (Hebrews 10:19-20)

Direct access. No human mediator standing between the believer and God. This is not a minor footnote — it's the reason a modern clergy class functioning as spiritual mediators between "regular" Christians and God is a step backward into a category Christ's priesthood already closed. The old covenant needed a priestly class because access was restricted. The new covenant abolished that restriction at the cross. Re-installing a mediating class — even informally, even with good intentions — argues against the very thing Hebrews spends ten chapters proving.

This doesn't erase the need for elders who teach, correct, and care for souls (that function is all over the New Testament, and we're not arguing otherwise). It erases the need for a priestly class — men who stand between the believer and God in a way Christ alone now does.

But Wasn't James the Head of the Church?

This is worth addressing directly, because it's the strongest historical counter-argument, and glossing over it would be dishonest.

James, the Lord's brother, clearly carries real weight in Jerusalem. Paul names him among the "pillars" of the church (Galatians 2:9). At the Jerusalem council, James speaks last and delivers the summary judgment: "Therefore my judgment is that we should not trouble those of the Gentiles who turn to God" (Acts 15:19). When Paul later returns to Jerusalem, he goes to see James, and "all the elders were present" (Acts 21:18).

So did Jerusalem have a head bishop, decades before the office supposedly developed?

Look closer at how the text actually presents it. The Jerusalem council's decision is never James acting alone — it comes from "the apostles and the elders, with the whole church" (Acts 15:22), and the resulting letter is sent in the name of "the brothers, both the apostles and the elders" (Acts 15:23). James speaks with recognized authority, but the decision and the letter are corporate. And when Paul visits in Acts 21, James is never alone — the elders are present with him, functioning as a body, every time Scripture puts James in a leadership scene.

There's also no process anywhere in Scripture for appointing a successor to "head of Jerusalem" the way there's an explicit process for appointing elders (Acts 14:23, Titus 1:5). If this were an office, we'd expect the text to show the office continuing past James. It doesn't. What we see instead looks like recognized personal standing — likely tied to James being a resurrection witness (1 Corinthians 15:7) and the Lord's own brother — operating within a plural eldership that never disappears around him.

That distinction matters, and it's an old and good one: respect for someone's maturity, wisdom, and demonstrated knowledge is not the same thing as installing them into a title that becomes an office others are formally appointed into. Every culture recognizes that some elders carry more weight than others because of experience or character. Scripture doesn't erase that. What it never does is turn James's personal standing into a title, a rank, or a hierarchy replicated in every church he never even visited.

The Honest Dilemma

None of this is a claim that structure is the enemy, or that plurality automatically produces something better. It's worth being honest about where this actually gets hard.

One pastor, reflecting on trying smaller home-based gatherings, said his church found the opposite of unity — cliques formed, and groups started comparing themselves to one another, one growing while another didn't. That's a real outcome, and it's worth sitting with rather than dismissing. But it's an argument about what sinful people can do to a good structure, not an argument that the structure itself was wrong. Man ruins good things. That's not a reason to abandon what Scripture actually calls for — it's a reason to expect it will be hard and require real character, not just a different room arrangement.

The same pastor has talked about the "potluck congregation" — people who show up for the softball team, the football watch party, the fellowship, all of it enjoyable and not wrong in itself, but absent the actual work of gathering around Scripture together. Fellowship is good. Softball is fine. But if that's substituted for the reason the church gathers in the first place — mutual edification through the word (1 Corinthians 14:26) — then the form survives while the substance is gone.

And there's a sharper example worth sitting with. A Bible study, structured well on paper — an elder opening with real substance, then breaking into smaller groups so everyone had room to participate. Good instincts. But the small group in question drifted into surface-level answers, uncorrected — Matthew 5:4, "Blessed are those who mourn, for they will be comforted," read as mourning over death rather than mourning over sin, with no one present able or willing to correct it. And when the room came back together and that same misreading was voiced out loud to everyone, it went uncorrected there too, even though the elder up front likely knew better. Plurality and participation, without anyone actually steering.

That's the dilemma stated plainly: rejecting a titled hierarchy does not mean rejecting qualified teaching. Plural eldership was never meant to mean untrained eldership, or a room where doctrine is simply whatever the group lands on. Scripture's own qualification for the role includes being "able to teach" (1 Timothy 3:2) and able to "rebuke those who contradict" sound teaching (Titus 1:9). A plurality of men who can't or won't do that isn't the New Testament model either — it's just a different kind of drift.

What this looks like, done well, is closer to a room of serious students gathered under several capable teachers than either a theater or a leaderless discussion circle. Multiple elders, rotating, teaching and engaging rather than performing. People free to raise real questions, wrestle with the text, and be heard. And someone present, every time, responsible enough and equipped enough to correct the room when it drifts into error — not to dominate the conversation, but to make sure the conversation stays anchored to what the text actually says.

Structure isn't the enemy. Titles, hierarchy, and a class of men elevated above "ordinary" believers are. What Scripture calls for is harder than either extreme — plural, functional, qualified leadership among the people, not a theater with an audience and not a room with no one steering the ship.

Where This Leaves Us

Peter was still called Peter. Paul addressed Timothy as Timothy, Titus as Titus — not by rank. The men who led the earliest churches were named, plural, and functioned among the people they served, not above them by title. What set them apart was character, maturity, and the ability to teach and correct — never a title that outlived the man or elevated him into a class of his own.

The church doesn't need bishops. It needs elders — plural, qualified, and known by name.

This isn't the easy path. Plurality without qualification drifts into the blind leading the blind. Qualification without plurality drifts right back into the very hierarchy we've just spent this whole document dismantling. Getting both right, at the same time, in a room full of imperfect people, is hard — and it will be done imperfectly, because man ruins good things. But the fact that it may fail at times is not a reason to abandon what Scripture actually calls for. It's a reason to expect the difficulty, and pursue it anyway.
