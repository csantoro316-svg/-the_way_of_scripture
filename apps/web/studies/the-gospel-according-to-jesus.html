The Gospel According to Jesus

Primary Translation: Berean Standard Bible (BSB)

There is a word that has been spoken so many times, in so many churches, across so many centuries, that most people assume they know what it means.

Gospel.

They have heard it from pulpits. They have seen it on signs. They have sung it in songs. And somewhere along the way, the word became familiar enough that the question behind it stopped being asked.

What is the gospel, actually?

Not what your tradition says it is. Not what you were handed as a child. Not what feels right or what seems loving or what makes the most people comfortable. What does Scripture say it is — in the language it was written in, in the world it was spoken into, from the lips of the ones who first proclaimed it?

That question is worth everything. Because the gospel, properly understood, is not one idea among many. It is the central fact of human history. And getting it wrong — even a little — changes everything that follows.

What the Word Actually Means

The Greek word translated "gospel" throughout the New Testament is εὐαγγέλιον (euangelion (yoo-ang-GEL-ee-on)). It means good news. But in the first-century Roman world, that phrase carried a weight most modern readers never feel.

Euangelion was not the word for a helpful idea or an inspiring teaching. It was the word used to announce a decisive event — a military victory, the birth of an emperor, the ascension of a new king. When a herald ran into a city shouting euangelion, he was not inviting people to consider a philosophy. He was proclaiming that something had happened — something irreversible, something that had changed the governing reality of the world — and that everyone in earshot now lived in the aftermath of it whether they knew it yet or not.

That is the word the New Testament uses for what Jesus brought.

Not a system. Not a set of principles. Not a framework for living a better life. An announcement. A proclamation. A declaration that a King had come, that He had conquered, and that nothing would ever be the same.

The Core — What Paul Says Is of First Importance

The clearest definition of the gospel in all of Scripture is given by Paul in 1 Corinthians 15. He does not leave the question open. He names it directly:

1 Corinthians 15:1-4 (BSB) "Now I want to make clear for you, brothers and sisters, the gospel I preached to you, which you received, on which you have taken your stand and by which you are being saved, if you hold firmly to the word I preached to you — unless you believed in vain. For I passed on to you as of first importance what I also received: that Christ died for our sins according to the Scriptures, that he was buried, that he was raised on the third day according to the Scriptures."

Four facts. Of first importance. Not peripheral. Not one consideration among many. Of first importance.

Christ died for our sins. He was buried. He was raised on the third day. This fulfilled Scripture.

That is the gospel. The death of the King as a sacrifice for sin. The burial that confirmed it was real. The resurrection that declared it was finished — and that the King who died now reigns. This is the euangelion. This is the announcement the herald brought. A new King has ascended. And His name is Jesus.

This is why Paul can say in Romans:

Romans 1:16 (BSB) "For I am not ashamed of the gospel, because it is the power of God that brings salvation to everyone who believes: first to the Jew, then to the Gentile."

Not the power of God that inspires people to be better. The power of God that brings salvation. Salvation from what? From sin and its consequence — death — and from separation from God that sin produces. The gospel saves because it announces that what sin demanded has been paid, and that the One who paid it has risen.

Ephesians 2:8-9 (BSB) "For it is by grace you have been saved through faith, and this not from yourselves; it is the gift of God, not by works, so that no one can boast."

Grace. Through faith. Not earned. Not deserved. Not produced by religious effort or moral progress or doctrinal correctness. Given. The gospel is not an achievement. It is a gift.

What the Gospel Is Not — And Why It Matters

There is a version of the gospel being preached in many places today that sounds close enough to be mistaken for the real thing, but isn't.

It goes something like this: the gospel is love. Jesus came to show us how to love each other. He welcomed the outcast, sat with sinners, and called us to do the same. That is the good news — that God loves you, that you should love others, and that love is the answer.

There is truth in that. Jesus did welcome the outcast. He did sit with sinners. He did call His followers to love in ways that scandalized the religious establishment of His day. Love is real, and central, and non-negotiable in everything that flows from the gospel.

But love without the cross is not the gospel. It is a sentiment. It has no power to deal with sin, no mechanism for forgiveness, no answer for death, no foundation for the command to love because it has no source of the love it commands. A gospel that is only love quietly removes the cross, makes the resurrection decorative, and turns Jesus from Savior and King into a moral teacher whose main lesson was kindness.

Paul could not be clearer about what happens when the gospel is altered:

Galatians 1:8-9 (BSB) "But even if we or an angel from heaven should preach a gospel contrary to the one we preached to you, let him be under a curse! As we have already said, so now I say again: If anyone is preaching to you a gospel contrary to the one you received, let him be under a curse!"

Twice. The same warning, repeated in consecutive verses. Paul does not say "be careful about alternate gospels." He says let them be accursed. The strength of that language tells you how high the stakes are. The gospel is not a starting point that can be refined or reframed or supplemented with better ideas. It is a fixed proclamation. Change it, and you have not improved it. You have lost it.

The core is the core. Everything else is built on top of it — or it is built on sand.

The Fullness — What Flows From the Core

And now the thing that must be said — the thing that most treatments of the gospel either miss entirely or say clumsily.

The core of the gospel is precise. Four facts. Of first importance. Christ died for our sins. He was buried. He was raised on the third day. This fulfilled Scripture.

But the gospel has a fullness that the core alone does not exhaust — not because the core is insufficient, but because of who the King is.

When a herald announced the ascension of a new king in the Roman world, the announcement was not just about the coronation. It was the beginning of a new era. The character of the king would determine the character of the kingdom. His values would shape the law. His heart would define what his reign meant for those who lived under it.

The gospel announces that Jesus Christ is King. And Jesus Christ is not a king who rules from a distance, demanding tribute from subjects He does not know. He is the King who laid down His life for the ones He loves. Who washed feet. Who touched lepers. Who called children to come to Him. Who wept at the grave of His friend. Who forgave the woman caught in adultery. Who told stories about a Father who saw His wayward son while he was still a long way off and ran to him.

Everything Jesus taught — every parable, every command, every word of the Sermon on the Mount — is not additional gospel. It is the character of the King made visible. It is what love looks like when it is not a sentiment but a Person. It is what the kingdom looks like when the One who reigns is not Caesar but Christ.

When Jesus says love your enemies, He is not adding a requirement to the gospel. He is showing you what the King is like — the One who loved His enemies enough to die for them while they were still His enemies (Romans 5:8).

When He says forgive seventy times seven, He is not giving a new rule. He is revealing the heart of the One who has forgiven an infinite debt that you and I could never repay.

When He says blessed are the poor in spirit, blessed are the meek, blessed are the merciful — He is describing the people who have understood the gospel well enough that it has begun to reshape them from the inside out.

The Sermon on the Mount is not the gospel. But it is what people look like when the gospel has done its work in them. Jesus is not describing how to earn the kingdom. He is describing what people look like when the kingdom has taken hold of them.

This is the fullness. Not a second gospel. Not gospel-plus. The single, unbroken reality of a King who is also a Savior, a Lord who is also a shepherd, a Judge who stepped into the judgment in the place of the condemned.

The gospel saves you from sin. And the King you receive when you are saved is Jesus — all of Him. And all of Him is more than any single proclamation can contain.

Receiving the Gospel

The response the gospel calls for has never changed.

Acts 2:38 (BSB) "Repent and be baptized, every one of you, in the name of Jesus Christ for the forgiveness of your sins. And you will receive the gift of the Holy Spirit."

Romans 10:9 (BSB) "If you confess with your mouth, 'Jesus is Lord,' and believe in your heart that God raised him from the dead, you will be saved."

Repentance. Faith. Confession. Baptism. These are not works that earn salvation — they are the natural response of a person who has heard the euangelion and understood what it means. The herald has run into the city. The King has come. He died for your sin. He rose from the dead. He reigns.

What will you do with that?

That is the only question the gospel leaves open. Everything else has already been decided — by a cross on a Friday, and an empty tomb on a Sunday morning, and a King who is alive and seated at the right hand of the Father, making intercession for all who come to Him.

John 3:16 (BSB) "For God so loved the world that he gave his one and only Son, that whoever believes in him shall not perish but have eternal life."

The gospel in a single sentence. God — the source. Love — the motive. The Son — the means. Belief — the response. Eternal life — the gift.

This is the good news. This is the euangelion. This is the gospel according to Jesus.

Have You Been Saved?

Everything above matters because the gospel is not merely something to understand. It is something that demands a response. If you have followed this study to this point, then the question now is not simply whether the message is clearer than it was before. The question is whether you have responded to the King it announces.

Scripture lays out plainly why salvation is needed, what God has done, and how a person must respond.

Romans 3:23 (BSB) "for all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God,"

Every person stands in that sentence. Scripture does not divide humanity into the openly broken and the quietly acceptable. All have sinned. All have fallen short. The ground at the foot of the cross is level.

Romans 6:23 (BSB) "For the wages of sin is death, but the gift of God is eternal life in Christ Jesus our Lord."

Sin does not wound lightly; it pays in death. But God does not answer death with another wage to be earned. He answers it with a gift: eternal life in Christ Jesus our Lord.

Romans 5:8 (BSB) "But God proves His love for us in this: While we were still sinners, Christ died for us."

God did not wait for you to become worthy, cleaned up, or ready. Christ died for sinners. The love that saves is not a reward for what you might become. It is the mercy of God moving toward you while you were still lost.

John 3:16 (BSB) "For God so loved the world that He gave His one and only Son, that everyone who believes in Him shall not perish but have eternal life."

This is why John 3:16 has never left the center of the Christian faith. It says in a single sentence what the human heart most needs to hear: God loved, God gave, Christ came, and everyone who believes in Him passes from death into eternal life.

Romans 10:9-10 (BSB) "if you confess with your mouth, 'Jesus is Lord,' and believe in your heart that God raised Him from the dead, you will be saved. For with your heart you believe and are justified, and with your mouth you confess and are saved."

Romans 10:13 (BSB) "for, 'Everyone who calls on the name of the Lord will be saved.'"

This is the response Scripture calls for: repent, believe, confess Jesus as Lord, and call on Him. To repent is not merely to feel bad or to say you are sorry. It is to turn: to turn away from sin, self-rule, and the life you were living apart from Him, and to turn toward Jesus Christ as Lord. Salvation is not inherited from family, achieved by moral effort, or secured by religious activity. It is received by turning to Jesus Christ in faith, entrusting yourself wholly to Him, and yielding to what He desires for your life.

If today is the day you are ready to do that, then call on Him. There is no magic wording that saves a person. The Lord is not listening for a formula. He is looking at the heart. But if you do not know how to begin, a prayer like this expresses what Scripture calls for:

Lord Jesus Christ, I know that I am a sinner and that I cannot save myself. I believe that You died for my sins and that God raised You from the dead. I turn from my sin and place my trust in You alone. Save me, forgive me, and take Your rightful place as Lord of my life. Teach me to follow You in truth, obedience, and faith. Amen.

And if this is your response, do not treat this as the end of something emotional, but as the beginning of something eternal. Read His Word. Obey what He says. Be baptized. Walk with His people. Follow Him not as a passing moment, but as the crucified and risen King who has called you out of death and into life. The old life is not the life you return to. Christ has opened the door of the kingdom to you. Step through it, and keep walking. You are not alone.
