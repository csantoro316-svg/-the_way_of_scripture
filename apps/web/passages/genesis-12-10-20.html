Passage

Genesis 12:10-20

Abram and Sarai in Egypt

Berean Standard Bible

10 Now there was a famine in the land. So Abram went down to Egypt to live there for a while because the famine was severe. 11 As he was about to enter Egypt, he said to his wife Sarai, “Look, I know that you are a beautiful woman, 12 and when the Egyptians see you, they will say, ‘This is his wife.’ Then they will kill me but will let you live. 13 Please say you are my sister, so that I will be treated well for your sake, and on account of you my life will be spared.”

14 So when Abram entered Egypt, the Egyptians saw that the woman was very beautiful. 15 When Pharaoh’s officials saw Sarai, they commended her to him, and she was taken into the palace of Pharaoh. 16 He treated Abram well on her account, and Abram acquired sheep and cattle, male and female donkeys, menservants and maidservants, and camels.

17 The LORD, however, afflicted Pharaoh and his household with severe plagues because of Abram’s wife Sarai. 18 So Pharaoh summoned Abram and asked, “What have you done to me? Why didn’t you tell me she was your wife? 19 Why did you say, ‘She is my sister,’ so that I took her as my wife? Now then, here is your wife. Take her and go!”

20 Then Pharaoh gave his men orders concerning Abram, and they sent him away with his wife and all his possessions.

Context

In Genesis 12, the Lord's call and promise to Abram begin the covenant story in earnest, yet the chapter also records Abram's failure under pressure in Egypt. Its movement from promise to fear to divine preservation shows both God's faithfulness and human weakness.

Common Interpretations

A common reading of this section is that Abram responds to pressure by fear-driven self-management rather than by trust. The famine is real, but the narrative spotlights both the deception he devises and the humiliating rebuke he later receives from Pharaoh. Interpreters often note that God's protection of Sarai does not vindicate Abram's strategy. The deliverance reveals divine faithfulness in the middle of human failure, and the whole episode sets up a later contrast with Isaac in Genesis 26.

Current Position

This site's current position is that Genesis 12:10-20 is not a model of wise initiative under pressure, but a picture of fear displacing trust. Abram had received God's promise, yet under famine he turns immediately to self-protection and deception. The point of the passage is not that the plan worked. It is that God remained faithful in spite of Abram's failure. That is why the later contrast with Isaac matters: similar hardship, but a different response when God's direction governs the decision.
