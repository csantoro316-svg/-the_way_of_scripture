Passage

1 Corinthians 3:11

LSB

"For no man can lay a foundation other than the one which is laid, which is Jesus Christ."

Context

In 1 Corinthians 3, Paul addresses a divided church that is thinking in worldly terms about leaders and status. The chapter moves from Christ as the only true foundation to the corporate people of God as His temple, so its warnings and promises concern the church's life in Christ rather than private spirituality detached from the body.

Explanation

The Church Jesus intended is not built on tradition, personality, or human institution, but on the living person of Christ. Paul's declaration affirms that no teaching, title, or structure can replace the bedrock of Jesus Himself. This mirrors early Christian gatherings, which were centered not around clergy or ornate buildings, but around the risen Christ — remembered in homes, by breaking bread, prayer, and mutual encouragement. It is not merely faith in teachings about Jesus that forms the foundation, but faith in Jesus as a living Lord, present among His people.

In contrast, many churches today have drifted from this foundation. In the Catholic Church, the elevation of the papacy and veneration of saints have, in practice, created additional layers between believers and Christ. The authority of tradition and magisterial teaching often competes with Scripture, making the institutional structure appear as immovable as the Gospel itself. Among Protestant denominations, Christ is affirmed doctrinally, yet the foundation is frequently expressed through formal confessions of faith — such as the Westminster or Augsburg Confessions — where theological systems, though valuable, can become the primary lens through which Christ is viewed. When loyalty to doctrine overshadows devotion to Jesus, the foundation shifts from the person of Christ to a framework about Him. In non-denominational churches, though less encumbered by formal tradition, the focus often shifts to charismatic leadership, worship experiences, or brand identity. Whether through hierarchy, heritage, or hype, the person of Jesus is too often displaced by structures that were meant only to serve Him. The early Church bore no such distractions — it gathered simply around Christ, and that simplicity was its strength.
