Passage

1 Corinthians 3:16

A core church passage: the people of God are His temple.

LSB

"Do you not know that you are the temple of God and that the Spirit of God dwells in you?"

Context

In 1 Corinthians 3, Paul addresses a divided church that is thinking in worldly terms about leaders and status. The chapter moves from Christ as the only true foundation to the corporate people of God as His temple, so its warnings and promises concern the church's life in Christ rather than private spirituality detached from the body.

Common Interpretations

Where once God's presence dwelt in the physical temple in Jerusalem, now He chooses to dwell in His people. This was a radical shift: worship was no longer geographically bound, but spiritually internal. This reality revolutionized worship in the early Church, which no longer needed altars or priests to mediate God's presence. Every believer is a mobile temple — a dwelling place for God. This calls for reverence not for buildings, but for the life within us. Just as the Spirit filled the tabernacle, He now fills human hearts — ordinary men and women made holy by His indwelling. Today, however, much of Christianity continues to function as though God's presence is confined to physical spaces or religious roles. In many expressions of the Catholic Church, the idea of sacred space persists through cathedrals, tabernacles, and the reserved Eucharist, reinforcing the perception that God dwells uniquely within consecrated buildings or objects. Many Protestant traditions replicate this mindset in subtler ways — elevating pulpits, platforms, or church buildings as "God's house," despite affirming the priesthood of all believers. In many non-denominational settings, the trend may be expressed through elaborate stage design, emotional atmospheres, or an emphasis on "experiencing God" in the worship space — often inadvertently reinforcing the idea that divine encounter is tied to the building or the event. Yet the early Church gathered in homes, courtyards, and open fields — not because they lacked options, but because they knew God's dwelling had moved into them. The modern Church must recover this truth: that God does not meet us at the altar, but abides within the altar of our hearts.
