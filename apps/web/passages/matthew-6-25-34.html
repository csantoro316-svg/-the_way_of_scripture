Matthew 6:25-34

Context

In Matthew 6, part of the Sermon on the Mount, Jesus contrasts hypocritical religion with true life before the Father. The chapter moves through giving, prayer, fasting, treasure, anxiety, and kingdom-seeking, so every individual saying must be read inside that unified call to trust the Father and seek His will first.

Common Interpretations

Here is the problem that has to be addressed honestly. Only fifteen verses after modeling a prayer that asks God for "daily bread," Jesus appears to say something very different — that His followers should not even be asking for food, drink, or clothing in the first place, because the Father already knows what they need. Read the full passage in your Bible (Matthew 6:25-34). Key verses are quoted below to walk through the argument. If the two passages are read at face value, side by side, there does seem to be real tension here. One passage models asking God for provision; the other tells us not to ask about provision at all, because the Father already knows and will take care of it. The resolution is not as simple as "pray for it, then stop worrying while you wait" — that reading makes verses 25-34 oddly redundant, since it takes Jesus fifteen verses and an extended illustration about birds and wildflowers to say something that could have been said far more briefly if that were really the only point. There is a meaningful clue in the Greek verb Jesus repeats in this passage.
