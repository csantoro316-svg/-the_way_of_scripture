Passage

Titus 1:5-9

Appointing Elders on Crete

Berean Standard Bible

The reason I left you in Crete was that you would set in order what was unfinished and appoint elders in every town, as I directed you. An elder must be blameless, the husband of but one wife, having children who are believers and who are not open to accusation of indiscretion or insubordination. As God’s steward, an overseer must be above reproach—not self-absorbed, not quick-tempered, not given to drunkenness, not violent, not greedy for money. Instead, he must be hospitable, a lover of good, self-controlled, upright, holy, and disciplined. He must hold firmly to the faithful word as it was taught, so that he can encourage others by sound teaching and refute those who contradict it.

Context

Paul turns from greeting to unfinished work in Crete. The issue is not creating a religious hierarchy for its own sake, but establishing mature local oversight able to guard teaching and shape community life faithfully.

Common Interpretations

This section is commonly read as showing that recognized leadership in the early church was real, but deeply character-driven. The qualifications focus less on status or charisma and more on proven life, household integrity, and doctrinal steadiness. Interpreters also commonly observe that the elder's role is both pastoral and protective. He must be able not only to encourage by sound teaching, but also to "refute those who contradict it."

Current Position

This site's current position is that Titus 1:5-9 confirms the early church had real local oversight, but not a priestly caste standing between believers and God. Elders are appointed servants charged with guarding teaching and shaping the life of the assembly. The passage also makes clear that qualification is moral and doctrinal before it is organizational. A man is not made fit for oversight by title, but by proven life and fidelity to the word.
