Passage

Mark 11:12-14

Full passage text for this reference is not yet quoted in the current local source material.

Context

In Mark 11, Jesus enters Jerusalem as king, judges the temple order, and uses the fig tree as a living sign of fruitless religion under judgment. The chapter's teaching on prayer is inseparable from that prophetic setting of faith, judgment, forgiveness, and divine purpose.

Common Interpretations

Jesus has just cursed the fig tree (Mark 11:12-14), and the disciples are marveling that it withered overnight (vv.20-21). The fig tree was not cursed because Jesus had a personal preference about figs. It was a deliberate prophetic act — a living parable of Israel's spiritual condition and coming judgment. Jesus was acting in complete alignment with what the Father had already purposed. The mountain-moving faith He then describes flows directly from that context — it is not faith that generates results from personal desire, but faith operating in alignment with God's sovereign purposes, the kind that moves what God has already determined to move. The Greek word translated "received" is ἐλάβετε (elabete (eh-LAH-beh-teh)) — and its form is significant. In Greek, this form describes a single, completed action — something already done and settled, not something still in process. "Believe that you received them" is not an instruction to generate enough belief until the result materializes. It is a description of the posture of someone resting in what God has already purposed — trust already placed, outcome already in His hands. The honest test of any reading of this verse is the rest of Scripture. If "all things for which you pray and ask" were an unconditional promise tied only to the strength of belief, Paul would have been healed of his thorn. Jesus Himself would have had the cup removed in Gethsemane. Neither happened — and in both cases the denial was purposeful and sovereign.
