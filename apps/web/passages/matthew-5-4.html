Common Interpretations

And there's a sharper example worth sitting with. A Bible study, structured well on paper — an elder opening with real substance, then breaking into smaller groups so everyone had room to participate. Good instincts. But the small group in question drifted into surface-level answers, uncorrected — Matthew 5:4, "Blessed are those who mourn, for they will be comforted," read as mourning over death rather than mourning over sin, with no one present able or willing to correct it. And when the room came back together and that same misreading was voiced out loud to everyone, it went uncorrected there too, even though the elder up front likely knew better. Plurality and participation, without anyone actually steering. That's the dilemma stated plainly: rejecting a titled hierarchy does not mean rejecting qualified teaching. Plural eldership was never meant to mean untrained eldership, or a room where doctrine is simply whatever the group lands on. Scripture's own qualification for the role includes being "able to teach" (1 Timothy 3:2) and able to "rebuke those who contradict" sound teaching (Titus 1:9). A plurality of men who can't or won't do that isn't the New Testament model either — it's just a different kind of drift. What this looks like, done well, is closer to a room of serious students gathered under several capable teachers than either a theater or a leaderless discussion circle. Multiple elders, rotating, teaching and engaging rather than performing. People free to raise real questions, wrestle with the text, and be heard. And someone present, every time, responsible enough and equipped enough to correct the room when it drifts into error — not to dominate the conversation, but to make sure the conversation stays anchored to what the text actually says.
