Passage

John 13:34-35

LSB

"A new commandment I give to you, that you love one another, even as I have loved you, that you also love one another. By this all men will know that you are My disciples, if you have love for one another."

Context

In John 13, Jesus prepares His disciples for His departure by washing their feet, exposing betrayal, and commanding them to love one another. The chapter frames Christian identity around the pattern and love of Christ Himself.

Explanation

Love is the identifying mark — not doctrine alone, not external success, not rituals. The early church shocked the Roman world by its sacrificial love: feeding widows, rescuing abandoned infants, and calling slaves brothers. Jesus' command goes deeper than sentiment — it demands a self-giving, patient, forgiving, inconvenient love. This love does not merely react; it initiates, serves, and sacrifices. When believers embody this love, the world recognizes something divine.

Today, however, love is often reduced to politeness, public image, or selective charity — far removed from the radical, relational love Jesus described. In the Catholic Church, institutional expressions of love — charities, hospitals, relief efforts — are widespread, yet often impersonal and bureaucratic, disconnected from the daily lives of ordinary believers. In many Protestant denominations, love is too often filtered through theological agreement — extended warmly to those who share the same doctrines, but withheld or weaponized against those who differ. Among non-denominational churches, love can be confused with friendliness, surface-level community, or emotional warmth during services, while genuine burden-bearing and accountability remain rare. In all camps, love has become something we claim to feel, rather than something we choose to do — especially when it's costly. The early Church's love was not efficient, polished, or programmed — it was messy, personal, and constant.
