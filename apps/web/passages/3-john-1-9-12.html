Passage

3 John 1:9-12

Diotrephes and Demetrius

Berean Standard Bible

I have written to the church about this, but Diotrephes, who loves to be first, will not accept our instruction. So if I come, I will call attention to his malicious slander against us. And unsatisfied with that, he refuses to welcome the brothers and forbids those who want to do so, even putting them out of the church. Beloved, do not imitate what is evil, but what is good. The one who does good is of God; the one who does evil has not seen God. Demetrius has received a good testimony from everyone, and from the truth itself. We also testify for him, and you know that our testimony is true.

Context

This is the conflict center of the letter. John contrasts selfish control with faithful witness and shows that leadership in the church can be tested by whether it serves the truth or exalts itself.

Common Interpretations

A common interpretation of Diotrephes is that he represents domineering leadership that refuses apostolic accountability. The problem is not merely a difficult personality, but a pattern of pride, exclusion, and resistance to truth. By contrast, Demetrius is usually read as a model of credible Christian character. John sets the two men side by side so readers can see how the fruit of a person's life confirms or exposes their claims.

Current Position

This site's current position is that 3 John 1:9-12 is one of the clearest warnings against self-exalting church leadership in the New Testament. Diotrephes does not model strength; he models the corruption that comes when a man loves to be first. Leadership in the church is validated by truth, hospitality, and character, not by control. That is why Demetrius stands as the positive counterexample.
