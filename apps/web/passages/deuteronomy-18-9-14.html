Passage

Deuteronomy 18:9-14

Sorcery Forbidden

Berean Standard Bible

9 When you enter the land that the LORD your God is giving you, do not imitate the detestable ways of the nations there. 10 Let no one be found among you who sacrifices his son or daughter in the fire, practices divination or conjury, interprets omens, practices sorcery, 11 casts spells, consults a medium or spiritist, or inquires of the dead. 12 For whoever does these things is detestable to the LORD. And because of these detestable things, the LORD your God is driving out the nations before you.

13 You must be blameless before the LORD your God. 14 Though these nations, which you will dispossess, listen to conjurers and diviners, the LORD your God has not permitted you to do so.

Context

In Deuteronomy 18, Moses distinguishes Israel sharply from pagan nations by forbidding occult and divinatory practices. The chapter sets covenant faithfulness against every attempt to seek spiritual guidance apart from the Lord's own appointed word.

Common Interpretations

A common reading of this section is that God forbids every attempt to seek hidden guidance by pagan or occult means rather than by His own revealed word. The prohibition is broad because the underlying impulse is broad. קֹסֵם (qosem (KOH-sem)) refers to a diviner, one who reads messages or guidance through signs, objects, or rituals. תּוֹעֵבָה (to'evah (toh-ay-VAH)) names something detestable or abominable to God, not merely old-fashioned or unwise.

Current Position

This site's current position is that Deuteronomy 18:9-14 does not merely ban a short list of ancient pagan practices. It exposes the deeper impulse behind them: the desire to obtain guidance, reassurance, or hidden knowledge apart from the way God has chosen to speak. That is why the passage bears directly on modern sign-seeking. Even when the form looks more respectable, the heart can still be reaching for confirmation by patterns, omens, and experiences instead of resting in what God has already made known.
