Passage

Acts 2:38

LSB

(LSB) "Repent and be baptized every one of you in the name of Jesus Christ for the forgiveness of your sins, and you will receive the gift of the Holy Spirit."

Context

In Acts 2, Pentecost marks the outpouring of the Spirit, Peter's proclamation of the risen Christ, and the first great response to the gospel. The chapter holds repentance, baptism, forgiveness, Spirit, and entry into the new covenant community together within the birth of the church.

Common Interpretations

The Greek preposition εἰς (eis (ays)) — translated "for" — is genuinely flexible, and on its own this verse could be read either way: be baptized in order to obtain forgiveness, or be baptized in connection with forgiveness already granted through repentance and faith. Grammar alone does not settle this verse. What settles it is the rest of Scripture. Acts 10 records the Holy Spirit falling on Cornelius and his household before baptism — Peter responds by baptizing those who already have the Spirit. If receiving the Spirit, and with it the forgiveness and new birth it represents, required baptism first, this sequence could not happen. So whatever Peter means here, it is not a fixed, mechanical formula where nothing spiritual occurs until the water. The better reading is that Peter is describing the whole conversion event as a single unit — repentance, forgiveness, baptism, and the Spirit, bound together as the normal pattern of becoming a Christian — not a strict checklist where step two unlocks step three. He is describing what happens together, using the language of aphesis (the releasing of the debt, accomplished through faith) alongside baptism (the washing, the apolouō, that follows it) because in the early church the two were never separated by more than a few hours.
