Passage

Luke 17:20-21

LSB

"Now having been questioned by the Pharisees as to when the kingdom of God was coming, He answered them and said, 'The kingdom of God is not coming with signs to be observed; nor will they say, "Look, here it is!" or, "There it is!" For behold, the kingdom of God is in your midst.'"

Context

In Luke 17, Jesus teaches about stumbling, forgiveness, faith, gratitude, and the coming of the kingdom. His words about the kingdom must be read against false expectations of spectacle and external signs alone.

Common Interpretations

Jesus rejected the notion that the kingdom would come with observable pomp or political takeover. Instead, He taught that the kingdom was a spiritual reality, alive within those who receive Him. The early Christians understood this not as escapism but as a reorientation: true power, peace, and authority reside in hearts aligned with God's reign. To live under His rule is to manifest the kingdom in our choices, relationships, and community. The world may still rebel, but within the believer, God reigns. In contrast, many modern churches still relate to the kingdom of God as if it were primarily an external structure — a political cause, a church program, or a measurable movement. In Catholicism, the Church itself is often presented as the visible manifestation of the kingdom on earth, with ecclesial authority and sacramental systems acting as its governing framework. Protestant denominations, while rejecting Rome's institutional claims, frequently build competing kingdoms through denominational expansion, influence in culture wars, or alignment with political ideologies. Even non-denominational churches, though unbound by formal systems, often equate the kingdom with visible growth — more attendees, more campuses, more "impact." This mirrors the very thing Jesus warned against: looking for the kingdom "here" or "there," rather than recognizing that it begins within the humble, surrendered heart. The early Church had no worldly platform or power, yet it turned the world upside down — not by building kingdoms, but by being one under the invisible rule of Christ. We must return to this inward reign if we hope to bear lasting fruit.
