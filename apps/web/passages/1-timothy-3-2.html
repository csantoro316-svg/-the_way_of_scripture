Common Interpretations

That's the dilemma stated plainly: rejecting a titled hierarchy does not mean rejecting qualified teaching. Plural eldership was never meant to mean untrained eldership, or a room where doctrine is simply whatever the group lands on. Scripture's own qualification for the role includes being "able to teach" (1 Timothy 3:2) and able to "rebuke those who contradict" sound teaching (Titus 1:9). A plurality of men who can't or won't do that isn't the New Testament model either — it's just a different kind of drift. What this looks like, done well, is closer to a room of serious students gathered under several capable teachers than either a theater or a leaderless discussion circle. Multiple elders, rotating, teaching and engaging rather than performing. People free to raise real questions, wrestle with the text, and be heard. And someone present, every time, responsible enough and equipped enough to correct the room when it drifts into error — not to dominate the conversation, but to make sure the conversation stays anchored to what the text actually says. Structure isn't the enemy. Titles, hierarchy, and a class of men elevated above "ordinary" believers are. What Scripture calls for is harder than either extreme — plural, functional, qualified leadership among the people, not a theater with an audience and not a room with no one steering the ship.
