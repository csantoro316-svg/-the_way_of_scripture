Passage

Matthew 6:11

Full passage text for this reference is not yet quoted in the current local source material.

Context

In Matthew 6, part of the Sermon on the Mount, Jesus contrasts hypocritical religion with true life before the Father. The chapter moves through giving, prayer, fasting, treasure, anxiety, and kingdom-seeking, so every individual saying must be read inside that unified call to trust the Father and seek His will first.

Common Interpretations

Once this connection is seen, the apparent contradiction between Matthew 6:11 and Matthew 6:25-34 dissolves, because both passages turn out to be saying the same thing from two different directions. "Give us this day our daily bread" is not, at its deepest level, a request for groceries — it is the cry of a soul declaring its dependence on Christ Himself as the only true sustenance. "Seek first His kingdom" in verse 33 is the same instruction stated plainly: seek Him, not provision, and everything else will be added as a consequence. The two passages stop competing once "daily bread" is understood as a request for continued life in Christ rather than a request for physical food. This is the language the daily bread petition is actually speaking. It is the prayer form of abiding — a daily, ongoing cry for continued union with the only source of true life, not a request directed at circumstances or material need.

  • Jerome (c. 347-420 AD): Jerome, translating the Gospels into Latin roughly a century and a half after Origen, made an unusual choice. In Luke's version of the Lord's Prayer he translated epiousios (eh-pee-OOH-see-os) with the ordinary Latin word for "daily." But in Matthew's version of the same Greek word, he instead coined a new Latin word that did not previously exist — supersubstantialem, "supersubstantial." Jerome explained his own reasoning directly: he understood this bread as bread "above all other substances, more excellent than all creatures" — and he identified it specifically with the body of the Lord. In other words, Jerome's choice of word was not a translation difficulty he stumbled into by accident. It was a deliberate theological judgment that the bread Jesus had in mind in Matthew 6:11 was not ordinary food at all, but Christ Himself. Like Origen before him, Jerome arrived at the same conclusion this document reaches through John 6 — working from the Greek text itself, more than a thousand years before anyone had reason to argue the point for any other purpose.

Both men were not guessing. Working directly from the Greek centuries before this study began, Origen and Jerome independently reached the same conclusion reached here through John 6 — that "daily bread" was never meant to be a request for food. This reading is not a modern reinterpretation forced onto the text. It predates the tradition that later flattened the verse into a simple request for material provision. Returning to the original language and the earliest readers does not introduce something new into the text — it recovers what was already there before tradition obscured it.
