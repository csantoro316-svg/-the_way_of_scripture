Context

1 Peter 5:3 should be read within the flow of its chapter and the larger argument of 1 Peter.

Common Interpretations

No longer is priesthood reserved for a lineage or a class — Christ has torn the veil. Every believer now has direct access to God and the authority to minister. The early church saw men and women praying, prophesying, healing, and teaching. The NT describes elders, overseers, and deacons — but as functional servant roles filled by spiritually mature believers, always plural, always character-based, and always described as serving among the people rather than presiding over them (1 Peter 5:3). What the NT does not describe is a single pastor as institutional head of a congregation, or a credentialed clergy class with exclusive access to spiritual authority. Spiritual gifts were distributed freely by the Spirit for the common good. This reclaims the dignity and responsibility of every Christian: to intercede, to bless, to proclaim truth. Institutional hierarchy is not what empowers ministry — faith and the Spirit do. Today, however, this truth has been largely abandoned or obscured. In the Catholic tradition, the priesthood remains highly stratified, restricted to celibate men, and seen as mediators between God and man — despite the torn veil at Calvary. The laity are often passive recipients, watching rather than participating. Many Protestant traditions, though rejecting the formal priesthood, still uphold an implicit divide through professional clergy systems, seminary credentialing, and rigid pulpit control. Many non-denominational churches, for all their informality, center spiritual authority in charismatic figures or worship teams, while the congregation remains largely disengaged. In each case, the radical, Spirit-led empowerment of the early Church has been replaced by structured control. But the biblical priesthood is not a title — it's a calling shared by all who are in Christ. It is worth acknowledging that passages like Ephesians 4:11-12, 1 Timothy 3, and Titus 1 do describe recognized leadership roles in the gathered community — pastors and teachers, elders and overseers, deacons. This document does not deny those roles or argue for a leaderless church. What it argues is that those roles, as the NT describes them, look nothing like what institutional Christianity developed them into after Constantine. Ephesians 4:11-12 says pastors and teachers exist to equip the saints for works of ministry — not to perform ministry on behalf of passive saints. That distinction is the point.
