Passage

1 Samuel 1:19-28

The Birth of Samuel

Berean Standard Bible

19 The next morning Elkanah and Hannah got up early to bow in worship before the LORD, and then returned home to Ramah.

And Elkanah had relations with his wife Hannah, and the LORD remembered her. 20 So in the course of time, Hannah conceived and gave birth to a son. She named him Samuel, saying, “Because I have asked for him from the LORD.”

21 Then Elkanah and all his house went up to make the annual sacrifice to the LORD and to fulfill his vow, 22 but Hannah did not go. “After the boy is weaned,” she said to her husband, “I will take him to appear before the LORD and to stay there permanently.”

23 “Do what you think is best,” her husband Elkanah replied, “and stay here until you have weaned him. Only may the LORD confirm His word.”

So Hannah stayed and nursed her son until she had weaned him.

24 Once she had weaned him, Hannah took the boy with her, along with a three-year-old bull, an ephah of flour, and a skin of wine. Though the boy was still young, she brought him to the house of the LORD at Shiloh. 25 And when they had slaughtered the bull, they brought the boy to Eli.

26 “Please, my lord,” said Hannah, “as surely as you live, my lord, I am the woman who stood here beside you praying to the LORD. 27 I prayed for this boy, and since the LORD has granted me what I asked of Him, 28 I now dedicate the boy to the LORD. For as long as he lives, he is dedicated to the LORD.”

So they worshiped the LORD there.

Context

In 1 Samuel 1, the narrative centers on Hannah's distress, her prayer before the Lord, and the birth of Samuel. The chapter is not merely about private desire being granted; it sets up God's larger work in Israel through the child who will become prophet, judge, and pivotal figure in the transition toward monarchy.

Common Interpretations

On the surface this looks like the clearest possible example of outcome-specific prayer working exactly as people assume it should — Hannah wanted a son, she asked God for a son, God gave her a son. But the full account of Hannah's prayer tells a more complicated and more honest story. What matters in the passage is not merely that a request was made and granted, but that the child asked for was immediately yielded back to the LORD and absorbed into God's larger purpose in Israel.

Current Position

This site's current position is that Hannah's story should not be used as a simple formula for obtaining desired outcomes. The request is inseparable from surrender, and the answer is inseparable from God's larger purpose for Israel. Samuel is not retained as a private possession proving a prayer method. He is given back to the LORD. That changes the force of the example: the passage shows dependence, vow, and divine purpose more than it shows a request system people can reproduce at will.
