Passage

Exodus 34:1-9

New Stone Tablets

Berean Standard Bible

1 Then the LORD said to Moses, “Chisel out two stone tablets like the originals, and I will write on them the words that were on the first tablets, which you broke. 2 Be ready in the morning, and come up on Mount Sinai to present yourself before Me on the mountaintop. 3 No one may go up with you; in fact, no one may be seen anywhere on the mountain—not even the flocks or herds may graze in front of the mountain.”

4 So Moses chiseled out two stone tablets like the originals. He rose early in the morning, and taking the two stone tablets in his hands, he went up Mount Sinai as the LORD had commanded him.

5 And the LORD descended in a cloud, stood with him there, and proclaimed His name, the LORD. 6 Then the LORD passed in front of Moses and called out:

“The LORD, the LORD God,

is compassionate and gracious,

slow to anger,

abounding in loving devotion and faithfulness,

7 maintaining loving devotion to a thousand generations,

forgiving iniquity, transgression, and sin.

Yet He will by no means leave the guilty unpunished;

He will visit the iniquity of the fathers

on their children and grandchildren

to the third and fourth generations.”

8 Moses immediately bowed down to the ground and worshiped. 9 “O Lord,” he said, “if I have indeed found favor in Your sight, my Lord, please go with us. Although this is a stiff-necked people, forgive our iniquity and sin, and take us as Your inheritance.”

Context

In Exodus 34, Moses returns to the mountain after the covenant breach, and the Lord proclaims His own name and character before him. Verse 8 belongs to that immediate revelation of divine holiness, mercy, and covenant faithfulness.

Common Interpretations

A common interpretation of this section is that Moses' response in verse 8 shows worship as immediate reverence before the revealed holiness of God. He does not perform for God; he falls before Him. This passage is often used to show that biblical worship is first a posture of surrender and awe. When the Lord's character is unveiled, the fitting human response is lowliness, not spectacle.

Current Position

This site's current position is that Exodus 34:8 reinforces the same pattern seen across Scripture: worship is the fitting human response to who God is, not a curated religious experience designed to produce a feeling. Moses bows because the LORD has revealed His name, holiness, mercy, and justice. Worship is therefore grounded first in divine revelation and only secondarily in any outward act that follows from it.
