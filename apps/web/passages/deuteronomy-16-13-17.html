Passage

Deuteronomy 16:13-17

The Feast of Tabernacles

Berean Standard Bible

13 You are to celebrate the Feast of Tabernacles for seven days after you have gathered the produce of your threshing floor and your winepress. 14 And you shall rejoice in your feast—you, your sons and daughters, your menservants and maidservants, and the Levite, as well as the foreigner, the fatherless, and the widows among you.

15 For seven days you shall celebrate a feast to the LORD your God in the place He will choose, because the LORD your God will bless you in all your produce and in all the work of your hands, so that your joy will be complete.

16 Three times a year all your men are to appear before the LORD your God in the place He will choose: at the Feast of Unleavened Bread, the Feast of Weeks, and the Feast of Tabernacles. No one should appear before the LORD empty-handed. 17 Everyone must appear with a gift as he is able, according to the blessing the LORD your God has given you.

Context

In Deuteronomy 16, the feast laws regulate how Israel is to gather before the Lord in the place He chooses. Verse 16 belongs to that larger pattern of appointed assembly, rejoicing, remembrance, and covenant allegiance.

Common Interpretations

A common reading of this section is that one obligation of worship under the old covenant was gathering before God at the appointed place and time. The emphasis is not private spirituality detached from assembly, but covenantal appearing before the Lord. Interpreters also note that no one was to appear empty-handed. The gathering itself carried the expectation of responsive offering and allegiance before God.

Current Position

This site's current position is that passages like Deuteronomy 16 matter because they show worship in Scripture has always included gathered, ordered, God-directed assembly. Private devotion has its place, but it is not a replacement for appearing before the Lord with His people. The old-covenant feast structure is not carried over unchanged into the ekklesia, but the underlying pattern still cuts against the modern reduction of worship to private feeling or passive attendance.
