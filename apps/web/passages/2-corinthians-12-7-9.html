Passage

2 Corinthians 12:7-9

LSB

"Because of the surpassing greatness of the revelations, for this reason, to keep me from exalting myself, there was given to me a thorn in the flesh, a messenger of Satan to torment me — to keep me from exalting myself! Concerning this I implored the Lord three times that it might leave me. And He has said to me, 'My grace is sufficient for you, for power is perfected in weakness.'"

Context

In 2 Corinthians 12, Paul moves from revelations to weakness, showing that divine power is displayed not through self-exaltation but through dependence on God's grace. The chapter reframes suffering and unanswered requests through the lens of God's purpose rather than human preference.

Explanation

This passage is instructive precisely because Paul is not a marginal example of weak faith — he is the apostle through whom much of the New Testament was written. He prayed for relief from real suffering, specifically and repeatedly, three times. God said no all three times. The reason was not arbitrary: the thorn served a purpose in Paul's life — keeping him from pride — that its removal would have undone. Paul could not see that purpose from where he stood. God could. The denial turned out to be the greater gift, and Paul received it as such rather than pressing for a different answer.

This is the deeper point Paul's thorn forces us to confront. If true faith means trusting that God already has a plan we cannot see and are not entitled to see, then repeatedly asking Him to change that plan is itself a sign our faith has not caught up to what we claim to believe. Paul knew how he was meant to live; he did not know God's specific plan for his suffering, and that not-knowing is the point. We are given what we need to know about walking with God — not what is coming, not why, not what He is doing through it. To keep asking Him to alter what we cannot see is to quietly admit we do not yet trust the One who can. Paul stopped asking — not because he ran out of words, but because he understood the asking itself stood between him and the grace already given.
