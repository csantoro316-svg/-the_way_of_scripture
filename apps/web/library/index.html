Central Study

The Gospel According to Jesus

There is a word that has been spoken so many times, in so many churches, across so many centuries, that most people assume they know what it means. Gospel. They have heard it from pulpits. They have seen it on signs. They have sung it in songs. And somewhere along the way, the word became familiar enough that the question behind it stopped being asked. What is the gospel, actually? Not what your tradition says it is. Not what you were handed as a child. Not what feels right or what seems loving or what makes the most people comfortable. What does Scripture say it is — in the language it was written in, in the world it was spoken into, from the lips of the ones who first proclaimed it? That question is worth everything. Because the gospel, properly understood, is not one idea among many. It is the central fact of human history. And getting it wrong — even a little — changes everything that follows.

Featured Study

Prayer and Faith

What Scripture Actually Teaches About How We Approach God

Most Christians inherit an idea of prayer rather than building one from Scripture. The common assumption is that prayer is a request system — you bring God a need or a desire, you ask sincerely, and if your faith is strong enough or your motives are pure enough, He grants it. When the answer doesn't come, people often reinterpret the outcome to fit what they prayed for, rather than asking whether they understood prayer correctly in the first place. This document works through what the Bible actually says prayer is — not what tradition has assumed it to be. The conclusion is more demanding than the popular view, but it is also more honest to the text: prayer is not a mechanism for securing outcomes. It is communion with God, submission to His will, and dependence on Christ Himself. Every passage below is examined in that light.

Featured Study

What Does Prayer Look Like?

What Scripture Models — From Jesus, Paul, and the Early Church

The question is not whether to pray. It is what prayer actually is. Scripture never describes prayer as a system for securing outcomes — ask sincerely enough, and the answer arrives. It describes something far more demanding: honest communion with the Father, total surrender to His will, and intercession that asks for the spiritual good of others rather than control over their circumstances. The clearest picture of what this looks like does not come from a theory about prayer. It comes from watching Jesus pray — especially in the moments when prayer cost Him the most.

Featured Study

Should We Seek Signs From God?

A Study on Coincidence, Confirmation, and Faith

Most of us have heard someone describe an experience like this: a particular bird shows up at just the right moment, a number keeps repeating, a dream feels too vivid to ignore, or something happens right after a prayer that seems to answer it exactly. The person telling the story usually isn't being dishonest or foolish — they're describing something that felt meaningful, often during a hard or emotional time, and they're reaching for language to make sense of it. "God sign," "god-wink," "coincidence that wasn't really a coincidence" — these are common ways people describe it. This study isn't about mocking that impulse or accusing anyone of doing something terrible. It's about asking a more basic question: does Scripture actually describe God working this way as the normal pattern for how He communicates with His people? And if not, what is this impulse, where does it come from, and what does Scripture say we should do with it? As we'll see by the end of this study, the honest answer is that this impulse is far more universal — and far more natural — than most of us realize. That includes the person writing this.

Featured Study

Baptism

What It Is, What It Does, and How It Should Be Done

Every question about baptism eventually comes back to a single Greek verb, and what it actually meant before any tradition got hold of it. The Greek word translated "baptize" throughout the New Testament is βαπτίζω (baptizō (bap-TID-zoh)). Its lexical meaning is to plunge, immerse, or submerge. In classical Greek it was used to describe dyeing cloth — plunging fabric into dye — and the sinking of ships. The word itself establishes full immersion as the original intent and the normative practice of the early church. The BSB renders the word consistently as "baptize" throughout — preserving the transliteration rather than the meaning, which is itself a translation choice worth knowing. The Greek is baptizō — to immerse or plunge — and the transliteration rather than translation of this word has shaped centuries of theological debate about mode. This matters because the mode of baptism is not arbitrary. The act of going fully under the water and rising out of it carries the entire theological picture baptism is meant to represent. The mode is the meaning.
