# The Way of Scripture — Project Doctrine & Standards

*This document governs all content decisions, translation choices, document structure, and development philosophy for thewayofscripture.com. It serves as the master reference for all content generation, site updates, and ongoing study.*

---

## Site Mission

The Way of Scripture exists to recover what Scripture actually says — in the language it was written in, in the context it was written for, before centuries of tradition, institutional pressure, and translation bias shaped what most people have been handed.

The site is not aligned with any denomination, tradition, or theological school. Its only authority is the original Hebrew and Greek text of Scripture, tested against the earliest available sources and the most reliable manuscript evidence. Every document on the site is a working document, subject to revision if the Word leads to a deeper or clearer understanding.

The site's name comes from Acts 9:2 — the earliest followers of Jesus were not called Christians. They were called followers of *The Way*.

---

## Core Philosophy

1. **The original text is the only inerrant standard.** Inerrancy belongs to the autographs — the original Hebrew and Greek manuscripts as God inspired them. No English translation is the final authority. Every translation is a human interpretive act.

2. **Man has been changing God's Word since Genesis 3.** Eve added "or touch it" — God never said that. The serpent built directly on that mishandling. Translation choices, institutional pressures, and doctrinal agendas have repeated this pattern throughout church history. The site documents this pattern without apology.

3. **The Berean standard is the operating method.** Acts 17:11 — the Bereans examined the Scriptures daily to see whether these things were so, even when Paul himself was teaching them. Every claim on this site is subject to that same examination. Nothing here is above it.

4. **Context is everything.** A verse pulled from its passage can mean the opposite of what the passage teaches. A passage read without its historical context can be applied to situations it was never addressing. The site always reads in full context, names the context, and never cherry-picks without acknowledging it.

5. **All major English translations agree on the core gospel.** The deity of Christ, His death and resurrection, salvation by grace through faith — these are not in dispute across translations. What the site examines are the downstream effects of specific translation choices on specific doctrines, where those choices have demonstrably shaped what people believe the text says.

6. **Scripture interprets Scripture.** No passage is interpreted in isolation. The full counsel of Scripture is the lens through which any individual verse is read.

## Translation Philosophy

### Primary Translation: Berean Standard Bible (BSB)

The Berean Standard Bible is the primary English translation used throughout the site. It was chosen for the following reasons:

- **Licensing:** The BSB is freely available for ministry use, including on websites, at no cost. This aligns with the site's commitment to making all reference content freely available to everyone.
- **Quality:** The BSB is a modern, readable, literal translation based on the best available manuscripts. Each word is connected back to the Greek or Hebrew source text.
- **Philosophy:** The BSB's name reflects the Acts 17:11 standard — the Bereans who examined Scripture daily — which is the operating method of this site.
- **Readability:** Unlike the ASV or KJV, the BSB uses clear modern English accessible to ordinary readers.

### The Original Text Remains the Benchmark

The BSB is the site's communication baseline — not its authority. Every theologically significant term is examined against the original Hebrew (Old Testament) and Greek (New Testament). Where the BSB rendering is insufficient, imprecise, or carries translation bias, the original word is named, explained, and its range of meaning established.

The Legacy Standard Bible (LSB) is used internally as a reference tool for comparison and for identifying translation nuances — particularly its restoration of the divine name *Yahweh* throughout the Old Testament. However, the LSB is never cited on the site due to licensing restrictions.

### On the Divine Name

No major English translation consistently restores the Hebrew divine name *YHWH* in a way that is both accurate and freely usable. Where the original text uses *YHWH*, the site notes this in the accompanying study text and explains the significance. The BSB renders *YHWH* as *LORD* in small capitals, consistent with standard English convention.

### On *Ekklēsia* and Other Key Terms

The BSB renders *ἐκκλησία* (*ekklēsia*) as "church" — the same translation decision made under King James' Rule 3 in 1611. This is addressed directly in the site's content on the church and in the inerrancy document. The translation choice in the BSB does not affect the site's argument; it simply means the argument must be made from the original Greek rather than from the translation. This is more consistent with the site's philosophy anyway.

## Document Standards

### Structure

- Every document is written in real explanatory prose — full paragraphs that introduce the question or tension before Scripture appears
- Bullets are reserved for genuinely listable items only, never as a substitute for explanation
- Every major section has a strong, deliberate opening and closing — no flat or generic openers/closers
- Scripture is always tied back in at section closes wherever possible

### Scripture Presentation

- **First reference:** Quote the full passage in a blockquote with reference and (BSB) notation
- **Long passages:** Add a "Read the full passage in your Bible" note before quoting key verses; never silently cherry-pick
- **Later references:** When a passage has already been quoted in full, cherry-pick only the relevant phrase on subsequent mentions — do not re-quote in full
- **Citation format:** Bold reference, italicized text in blockquote

### Greek and Hebrew Terms

- Every theologically significant Greek or Hebrew term is presented with:
  - The original script: e.g., *βαπτίζω*
  - The transliteration in italics: *baptizō*
  - A plain-English pronunciation guide in parentheses with capital letters marking the stressed syllable: *(bap-TID-zoh)*
  - A plain-English definition
- Pronunciation guides use intuitive spellings — "OOH" not "OO" or "ew" for the oo sound; "KH" for the guttural Hebrew ch sound
- Terms are defined on first introduction; subsequent references use the term without re-defining

### Language

- Real, direct, powerful language — never mechanical, technical, or reaching for cleverness
- No contrastive strawman phrasing unless Scripture itself raises the contrast
- Plain language over academic or theological jargon
- No hedging on established positions; acknowledge genuine tensions honestly rather than smoothing them over

### Internal Consistency

- Before finalizing any document, check for internal conflicts — places where an earlier framing that has since been revised still lingers in later sections or the closing summary
- Theological positions established in one document are maintained consistently across all documents

### Standard Closing Statement

Every document closes with the following statement (formatted in italics):

*The understanding presented here reflects our best study of God's Word at this time. Scripture calls us to come together as the ekklēsia — the called-out assembly — and let all things be done for edification. (1 Corinthians 14:26) Your thoughts, questions, and insights are not just welcome — they are part of this process. Iron sharpens iron. If the Word leads us to a deeper or clearer understanding through your contribution, this site will be updated to reflect it. Your voice matters here. This is not a finished work. It is a table we are all sitting at together.*

## Content Philosophy

### What the Site Does

- Examines Scripture in the original languages with full context
- Documents historically verified translation choices and their consequences
- Provides reference material that people can test against Scripture themselves
- Invites engagement, questions, and correction — all documents are working documents

### What the Site Does Not Do

- Align with any denomination, tradition, or theological movement
- Claim final authority on any question Scripture leaves open
- Cherry-pick without acknowledging the full context
- Present inferences as textual conclusions — these are always distinguished

### On Denominational Characterizations

- When describing tendencies in Catholic, Protestant, or non-denominational churches, use "many" as a qualifier rather than universal claims
- Target systems and patterns, not entire traditions
- Acknowledge where communities practice what Scripture describes, even while naming where the broader pattern departs from it

### On Scholarly Consensus

- Scholarly consensus is not the standard. The original text is the standard.
- Where scholarly consensus agrees with the text, cite it as confirmation
- Where scholarly consensus departs from the text or is driven by institutional or doctrinal pressure, name that plainly
- The site applies the same scrutiny to its own arguments that it applies to inherited tradition

## Gospel Engagement Principles

*(For use in conversations about faith — with Jeff, with family, in study groups)*

1. **Scripture first.** Always redirect to what the text actually says. "What does this passage say?" is the master question.

2. **Question-driven.** Frame challenges as invitations to examine Scripture together, not as accusations or corrections.

3. **Stay in the passage.** When a conversation partner responds with what they think rather than what the text says, bring it back: "Can you show me where this passage says that?"

4. **Don't defend the method — demonstrate it.** Explaining the Socratic approach at length is less effective than simply using it. The method proves itself when the text lands.

5. **The Berean standard turned back.** When someone says you're cherry-picking: "Which part of this passage do you think I'm misreading? Let's read the whole thing together right now."

6. **When they go beyond the text:** "You may be right that Scripture addresses that elsewhere — but what does this passage say? Let's stay here first."

7. **Spirit-dependent.** Conversion and understanding are the Spirit's work. The role is faithfulness to the text, not winning. Plant the seed. The Spirit does the rest.

8. **On sheep and borrowed belief:** "If you're not willing to dig into Scripture yourself and show me what it actually says — you're doing exactly what the world does. Following what someone else told you. What makes that any different?"

9. **The LDS redirect applies everywhere:** The questions used with LDS missionaries — "where does the Bible say that?", "where did the early church practice this?" — apply equally to any inherited assumption about prayer, worship, church structure, or the nature of salvation.

10. **Love and truth together.** Never compromise truth. Never lose the relationship. The goal is the person, not the argument.
